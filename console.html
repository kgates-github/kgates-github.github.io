Tl; dr
  • For enterprise developers, getting access to software-building tools on cloud platforms is rife with bottlenecks and red tape. We reimagined a better experience by anchoring product ideas around a nuanced understanding of customer problems.
  • Our solutions were not just informed by the fact that bottle necks and red tape exist, but by why they exist.
Impact
  • Our product vision was the foundation for a compact, two-day design sprint and product kickoff.
  • Our research had an impact far beyond the scope of this project—it informed several other teams working in a similar domain.
Learnings
  • If a design process is not working, nip it in the bud and pivot.
  • Transdisciplinary ideation is energizing and fruitful.
  • Storyboards are a powerful way to convey product visions.
A Human-Centered Approach to Enterprise Problems
A Big Ask
This project started as a challenge posed by Pivotal's director of product for its London R&D org. She wanted me to do two things:
  1. Discover the top problems engineers in large enterprises have in getting the tools they need to build software.
  2. Organize a product team anchored around those problems and come up with solutions.
It was a big ask, but also a big opportunity.
The Challenge
My Role
As design lead, I drove the research and synthesis over a period of several weeks. After that, I facilitated a week-long ideation workshop and design sprint.
Skills
User research, workshop facilitation, collaborative ideation, storyboarding
Collaborators
For the research, I paired with an engineer (and aspiring designer) on a daily basis. After that, I facilitated design workshops which included an R&D engineering team, a PM, and a new-hire designer. I also had weekly one-on-ones with the director of product.
Roadmapping
A Collaborative Product Roadmap
We prioritized team work by first understanding the problems we faced. I facilitated a workshop in which engineers, PMs and design formed open questions around our central theme of developer tool access. We then prioritized problems and brainstormed activities.

Research
User Interviews
Discovering the Reality of the Enterprise Developer Experience
In our roadmapping workshop, the concensus was that we needed to learn more about the app developer experience.
We interviewed around 30 customers, non-customers, and members of our consulting business who work on-site. The interviews were split between app devs and platform operators.
We wanted to find out things like “How do developers decide what tools they need?” and “What do they find frustrating?”
Outline of a discussion guide. We start with learning goals, then organize them into sections.
We turned our outline into a discussion guide by figuring out how to actually ask questions (e.g., "Tell me about the last time...")
Making Synthesis a Team Effort
We rotated teammates onto the synthesis process so they could see for themselves how insights emerge from data. There are key benefits to doing this:
  • Transparency: Having teammates rotate onto design activities lets them see how we discover patterns. That builds credibility, which then builds trust in the data and paves the way for evidence-based decision making.
  • Alternate perspectives: We were doing research in a very technical domain. Engineers were in a good position to help us find patterns we might not have found ourselves.
Once our affinity map started taking shape, we asked for volunteers to help. In this picture, the engineer I collaborated with described affinity mapping to another engineer as “optimization through evolution”.
Our affinity map organized our findings and captured the essence of the end-to-end experience.
Personas
Understanding Our Two Primary Users
App Developer
Cody
Role: App developers build apps and other types of software.
Characteristics: 97 to 99 percent of our customers’ developers are early in their careers. Most are focused on writing code and are not comfortable working with cloud infrastructure. App devs work on small teams in large engineering departments that can range in size from hundreds to tens-of-thousands.
Key Driver: App devs want access to a wide variety of tools and the latest hot tech.
Underlying Motivators: There are a number of reasons driving app dev demand for tools:
  • Role-specific needs: Developers come in many forms. A data scientist requires a very different set of tools than a mobile app developer.
  • Task-specific needs: Engineers sometimes start with specific needs related to the task at hand. For example, an aerospace engineering team creating flight control tests might need a time-series database with specified level of latency.
  • Resume value: Developers often want the latest hot tech. This is partly due to resume value, which can determine their viability in the job market.
Platform Operator
Alana
Role: Platform operators maintain the cloud platform. They are responsible for providing tools for developers, keeping the platform up-to-date, and troubleshooting when something goes wrong.
Characteristics: Even in large organizations, operator teams tend to be small, with 10 - 20 people. This contrasts with the fact that their platforms can be used by hundreds, even thousands of developers.
Key Driver: Operators want to offer a small, manageable set of tools that cover a wide surface area.
Underlying Motivators: Despite app developer demands, operators are often unwilling or unable to provide all of the tools developers want. There are a number of reasons for this:
  • Risk aversion: Cloud platforms are extremely complex, but tend to be stable as long as nothing changes. Introducing new tools means introducing change, and change brings risk of something going wrong.
  • Developing expertise: If an operations team offers a new technology on their platform, they are expected to be experts at that technology. When something goes wrong, the operations teams are expected to fix it.
  • Not enough demand: Operations teams are inherently small and they tend to be unwilling to support tools for which there is little demand.
Key Findings
Our Primary Personas Are Fundamentally Misaligned
At the heart of everything was a fundemental conflict: While app developers want a wide variety of tools and the latest, hot technology, platform operators want to offer a small, manageable set of tools.
Seeing the Journey as a Series of Conflicts Between Personas
We visualized the misalignment between personas as a cartoon argument. We then mapped the argument onto a skeleton version of the journey. That conveyed where on the journey misalignment can lead to bottle necks and frustration.
In our research findings presentation, we imagined a series of fictional arguments between personas to convey misaligned interests.
Dev Tool Access Patterns
One way or another, developers tend to get the tools they need, often through off-platform sources. Some of these, such as dedicated service teams, are sanctioned, while others, such as developers going "DIY", are not, which can lead to security and compliance risk.
Diagrams from our research findings deck that depict the various ways developers get the tools they need from a variety of sources.

Workshop Prep
Feedback as a Forcing Function
Before ideation week began, I set up sources of feedback and embedded them into the whole process. Getting continuous feedback would help keep us sane as we navigated a vast problem space.
Panel of advisory experts
I recruited a panel that included people from related product teams, field engineers and marketing from Europe and North America. I set up two sessions with them on Wednesday and Friday, and presented our progress.
The panel brought relevant and diverse perspectives to the table.
Field engineers
Several field engineers volunteered their time. We included them in activities and asked them to evaluate early ideas.
Field engineers are great because they often work on-site for months at a time and can bring the customer perspective to the table.
Open doors
I invited the whole Pivotal office to stop by any time. When anyone showed up, one of us would take the lead and explain our current thinking, and how we got there.
Random visits made sure we could clearly articulate everything we were doing at any time.
Articulating concepts clearly requires thinking clearly and is refined through practice. We built that practice into the design process.

Determining Where We Can Have the Most Impact
With many customer problems at hand, we needed to decide which ones to tackle. We used three techniques:
  • Surveying our field engineers to assess where we can impact customer needs.
  • Determining where we, as a product team, can move the needle.
  • Assessing any engineering risks.

Five Whys
With help from the field, we took our top problems and explored root causes by asking ourselves "why?" as many times as possible. This would help us:
  • See relationships between problems and have a more holistic perspective.
  • Identify big pain points that lead to a lot of down-stream problems.
  • Exanded the field of potential problems we could solve.

Product Vision
Tying the Story Together
Scenario writing session
Scenario Writing
On Thursday, we took our top ideas and wrote scenarios describing a future in which our customers are enjoying the value of our not-yet-existent products. We were envisioning presenting a storyboard to the advisory panel on Friday and wanted our ideas to be approachable and expressive.
A Misfire and Redress
“What is the point of scenario writing? Have we learned anything?”, asked an engineer on Friday morning. I probed the room. “What do we think?”, I asked.
In short, our solution ideas were not solid enough technically for our engineers. For a lot of types of projects, safe assumptions can be made about the tech. That was not the case for us.
We went back to the whiteboard and discussed. The solution was to group ideas into product concepts. Those allowed the engineers to envision the "how" to a level that was acceptable.
Our scenario writing didn't work out as planned. We would need to improvise.
Idea Boards and Advisory Panel Feedback, Round Two
We still needed something to present to the advisory panel. We came up with idea boards. Idea boards, which were based on our product concepts, tied together outcomes, evidence and personas.
We created an idea board for each of our top concepts and presented to the advisory panel for feedback.
Our improvised idea boards conveyed concepts and tied together outcomes, data and personas.
Independent Service Offerings
Outcome: Operators can delegate the curation and management of services to non-operators, effectively increasing the offerings.
Different Levels of Support
Outcome: Developers can provision a wider variety of tools that are not mission-critical (such as databases with lower uptime) and are managed by non-operators.
Automated Tool Integration Tests
Outcome: Third-party tool providers have increased confidence in an integration before releasing it. Operators have increased confidence in an integration before managing it.
Conclusion
Impact
Pivotal was acquired by VMWare early 2020 and our teams were reorganized, so this project did not have the ending I would like. Despite that, we still had impact.
Design Sprint
Our ideation session was the foundation for a compact, two-day design sprint. (I plan to include the sprint as a separate case study.)
Research Impact
Our research deck got a lot of attention. I presented it several times as part of our integration process. At several points there were dozens of people in the Google doc, including vice presidents. One PM told me she had been referring to the deck on a regular basis.
In addition, I later used the research findings to inform two other releated projects.
Design Practice Impact
I documented the design process and presented it internally and externally, including two times for the U.K. Government Digital Services.
Learnings
If something is not working, nip it in the bud and pivot:
  • Our initial scenario-based presentation didn't gel. We took the outcome we were after and came up with a solution that worked.
  • High trust enables critical feedback, which was necessary for us to recover from mistakes.
Transdisciplinary ideation is powerful:
  • We gained insights that only came about because designers, engineers and field engineers were working together on the same problem.
  • Marketing teams members on our advisory panel had feedback that rest of us would not have thought of.
Don't underestimate the impact of vision storyboards:
  • I got feedback that people had no idea of what we were up to until they saw the storyboard.
  • In large organizations, I've found people will hear about your project, assume worst-case scenario, and imagine confict that doesn't exist. A good vision storyboard can dispell mistrust and open doors for collaboration.
