Pivotal Developer Console

How I guided a balanced team through an ambitious effort to reimagine how engineers in large enterprises get the tools they need to build software.

Entering the world of red tape and shadow IT...

Pivotal’s main product is a cloud-based operating system that makes it easy for developers to deploy apps on platforms like Amazon Web Services.

Our customers are Fortune-500 companies who each employ thousands of engineers, all of whom need a diverse set of tools, such as databases and APIs to do their jobs. Getting access to these tools is rife with bottlenecks, red tape and shadow IT.

Challenge

Shortly after I arrived in Pivotal’s London office, I met with the director of product, who posed a challenge: She wanted me to discover the top problems enterprise engineers have in getting the tools they need and anchor a product team around them.

It was a big challenge. I was in London to hire and build out a design team, which meant I was the only designer in town, at least for a while.

My Role

As design lead, I drove the research and synthesis over a period of several weeks. After that, I facilitated a week-long ideation workshop and design sprint.

Collaborators

For the research, I paired with an engineer (and aspiring designer) on a daily basis for a couple months. I also facilitated various design workshops that included engineers, PMs, stakeholders, marketing, and others.

Design Roadmapping
We prioritized design work by first understanding the problems we faced.

I began everything by facilitating a workshop in which engineers, PMs and design formed open questions around our central theme of developer tool access. We grouped the questions into themes, dot voted, and discussed. We later brainstormed activities and prioritized them.

Why create a design roadmap?

  • Design roadmapping help the team collectively agree on what problems are most important and if any are urgent.

  • Designers (myself included), can be biased towards tackling problems we happen to know how to solve.

  • Design should meet the project where it is. If, say, the PM is anxious about an upcoming BETA release, we might give that our attention first.

  • Design roadmaps are a great way for designers to build personal growth into their day-to-day. They are free to suggest design activities that they have never done.

An example of a design roadmap (from a more recent project) that shows activities, priority and status.
Interviews
We started with a question: “What’s it like for engineers in large enterprises to get the tools they need to build software?” .

We started our discussion guide with questions from our design roadmapping, then brainstorming more open questions around our central inquiry. We wanted to find out things like “How do developers decide what tools they need?” and “What do they find frustrating?”

We then grouped the questions by theme, then crafted the actual questions asked in the interviews (e.g., “Tell me about the last time…”). We did this so we could include important technical questions posed by engineering, along with our more broad objectives without introducing bias.

We interviewed around 30 customers, non-customers, and members of our consulting business who work on-site. The interviews were split between app devs and platform operators.

Crafting a discussion guide with questions from many viewpoints including engineering, product and design.
Synthesis
Show, don’t tell: We rotated teammates onto the synthesis process so they could see for themselves how insights emerge from data.

Once our affinity map started taking shape, we asked for volunteers to help. In this picture, the engineer I collaborated with explained affinity mapping to another engineer as “optimization through evolution”.

In an engineering-centric culture like Pivotal’s, co-workers who have not worked with designers can be skeptical about our processes. I’ve found having teammates rotate onto design activities builds credibility, which then builds trust in the data and paves the way for evidence-based decision making.

We were also doing research in a very technical domain, so engineers were in a good position to help us find patterns we might not have found ourselves.

The Central Conflict
There are fundamentally misaligned interests between app developers and platform operators.

App developers want a wide variety of tools and the latest, hot technology. Platform operators want to offer a small, manageable set of tools.

Any solutions we would come up with would have to account for these misaligned interests. To help with this, we created a fictional set of arguments between an app dev and operator. Our goal was to drive home key points in a memorable way.
