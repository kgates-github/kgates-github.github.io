Pivotal Developer Console

How I guided a balanced team through an ambitious effort to reimagine how engineers in large enterprises get the tools they need to build software.

Entering the world of red tape and shadow IT...

Pivotal’s main product is a cloud-based operating system that makes it easy for developers to deploy apps on platforms like Amazon Web Services.

Our customers are Fortune-500 companies who each employ thousands of engineers, all of whom need a diverse set of tools, such as databases and APIs to do their jobs. Getting access to these tools is rife with bottlenecks, red tape and shadow IT.

Challenge

Shortly after I arrived in Pivotal’s London office, I met with the director of product, who posed a challenge: She wanted me to discover the top problems enterprise engineers have in getting the tools they need and anchor a product team around them.

It was a big challenge. I was in London to hire and build out a design team, which meant I was the only designer in town, at least for a while.

My Role

As design lead, I drove the research and synthesis over a period of several weeks. After that, I facilitated a week-long ideation workshop and design sprint.

Collaborators

For the research, I paired with an engineer (and aspiring designer) on a daily basis for a couple months. I also facilitated various design workshops that included engineers, PMs, stakeholders, marketing, and others.

Design Roadmapping
We prioritized design work by first understanding the problems we faced.

I began everything by facilitating a workshop in which engineers, PMs and design formed open questions around our central theme of developer tool access. We grouped the questions into themes, dot voted, and discussed. We later brainstormed activities and prioritized them.

Why create a design roadmap?

  • Designers (myself included), can be biased towards tackling problems we happen to know how to solve. Design roadmaps put problems first ans solutions second.

  • Design should meet the project where it is. If, say, the PM is anxious about an upcoming BETA release, we might give that our attention first.

  • Design roadmaps are a great way for designers to build personal growth into their day-to-day. They are free to suggest design activities that they have never done.

An example of a design roadmap (from a more recent project) that shows activities, priority and status.
Part 1
Research
Interviews
We started with a question: “What’s it like for engineers in large enterprises to get the tools they need?”

We started our discussion guide with questions from our design roadmapping, then brainstorming more open questions around our central inquiry. We wanted to find out things like “How do developers decide what tools they need?” and “What do they find frustrating?”

We interviewed around 30 customers, non-customers, and members of our consulting business who work on-site. The interviews were split between app devs and platform operators.

Show, don’t tell. We made synthesis a team effort.

User Roles

App developers

Key attributes:

App developers build apps and other types of software.

Ninety-seven to ninety-nine percent of our customers’ developers are early in their careers. Most are focused on writing code and are mostly not able or comfortable working with cloud infrastructure.

Key drivers:

App devs generally want access to a wide variety of tools and the latest hot tech. There are a number of reasons for this:

Diversity of roles

Developers come in many forms. A data scientist requires a very different set of tools than a mobile app developer.

Resume value

Developers often want the latest hot tech. This is partly due to resume value, which can determine their viability in the job market.

Specific needs

Engineers sometimes start with specific needs. For example, they might need a time-series database with specified level of latency.

“The problem with [developer tools] is like the problem with the word processor: Out of hundreds of features, only 10 percent are used, but everyone uses a different 10 percent.”

Platform operators

Key attributes:

Platform operators maintain the cloud platform. They are responsible for providing tools for developers, keeping the platform up-to-date, and troubleshooting when something goes wrong.

Even in large organizations, operator teams tend to be small, with 10 - 20 people. This contrasts with the fact that their platforms can be used by hundreds, even thousands of developers.

Key drivers:

Operators want to offer a small, manageable set of tools that cover a wide surface area. Despite app developer demands, operators are often unwilling or unable to provide all of the tools developers want. There are a number of reasons for this:

Risk aversion

Operators tend to let sleeping dogs lie. Cloud platforms are extremely complex, but tend to be stable as long as nothing changes. Introducing new tools means introducing change, and change brings risk of something going wrong.

Developing expertise

If an operations team offers a new technology on their platform, they are expected to be experts at that technology. When something goes wrong, the operations teams are expected to fix it.

Not enough demand

App dev teams sometimes have unique needs. Operations teams are inherently small and they tend to be unwilling to support tools for which there is little demand.

Remembering getting burned

Consequences for problems with the platform can be severe. Downtime could mean lost revenue. A security breach might bring bad press and subpoenas. Audit teams can appear unannounced at any time, and if they uncover mistakes, shut things down for months.

“An audit team found unencrypted passwords on a VM and shut the operations team down for eight months.”
Consequences
One way or another, app devs get what they want, but often with hidden costs.

When operators are unwilling to offer tools on their platform, there are several ways developers get what they want:

  • Developers go DIY

    Developers sometimes just install what they want by themselves. This can happen when operators are unwilling to provide a service they want, but developers are also frustrated by red tape. It can take up to six months to get access to a database at some organizations.

    Consequences:

    This leads to down-stream risks such as non-compliance, reduced security and shadow IT. Developers also might not have the skills needed to maintain databases and other tools at scale, which can lead to catastrophe. Shadow IT, or, IT that emerges “off-the-grid”, is inherently inefficient.

  • Dedicated service teams

    Some customers have dedicated teams devoted to one service, such as a particular kind database.

    Consequences:

    Service teams are often off-platform and therefore don’t benefit from the cost-savings, redundancy and other factors gained by being hosted on-platform.

    Service teams are largely sanctioned and could potentially solve

  • Hosted services

    Some companies, such as Oracle, can host and manage their databases for customers.

    Consequences:

    This tends to be expensive and might not work for companies in highly-regulated industries that need to host data on-premises or have other special needs.

Part 2
Ideation Week
Kickoff
It was a Monday morning. We had no idea where we would end up by Friday.

Our mandate was to explore customer problems around developer tools access, understand what the most important problems are, and come up with a product. It was daunting.

When entering into an open-ended, ambiguous problem space like this, there are few things that can help:

  • Data: We surrounded ourselves with data. The journey map and key slides from our research deck were up on all the walls around us.

    Conversations anchored around data tend to be fruitful. Establishing that everyone needs to back their assertions with facts helps teammates avoid taking stances. Taking stances can lead to interpersonal conflicts and undermine collaboration.

  • Feedback: Feedback was constant and hugely important (see the next section).

  • Team health checks: I ran quick health checks everyday to get a sense if anything was not okay and needed attention.

We printed out our journey map and kept it close by to use as a reference. Many conversations started with us huddled around it.

Printouts underscoring the tensions between key personas, which was central to our research findings, were always around.
Embedding Feedback
We didn’t invite criticism. We imposed it.
Long before ideation week began, I had set up sources of feedback and embedded them into the whole week-long process. We made feedback a forcing function.

Why?

  • Sanity: In a huge, week-long, open-ended ideation session, it’s impossible to imagine the product direction you’ll come up with by Friday. It was much easier to focus on what we needed to present to our advisory panel on Wednesday afternoon (see below).

  • Clarity in thinking: Articulating concepts clearly means thinking clearly. Thinking clearly requires practice. We built that practice into the design process.

  • Countering group-think: I sometimes worry that ideation workshops can create a group-think bubble. Constantly exposing what we were doing to people outside our immediate sphere brought scrutiny to everything we did.

This is how we prearranged several sources of feedback:
An advisory panel feedback session.

Random visits made sure we could clearly articulate everything we were doing at any time.

Prioritizing Problems
We first asked ourselves, “Where can we have the most impact?”
With many customer problems at hand, we needed to decide which ones to tackle. We used three techniques:

Field survey

We asked field engineers to choose the problems that, if solved, would have the highest impact on their customers. This helped us stay connected to customer needs.

Wheelhouse check

We voted on what problems could be tackled by an engineering-centric product team. We cared about problems outside our wheelhouse, as problems are often intertwined, but wanted to focus on where we would have the most impact.

Engineering gut check

Engineers estimated potential complexity at the outset. We wanted to avoid problems that would take months to solve, and/or have high technical risk.

We surveyed our field team to get a ranking of problems and then sorted them into columns based on how much impact a product team would have on them.
Engineering gut check. We mapped customer problem statements by potential complexity and impact.
By noon the first day, we had honed in on a handful of customer problems that were both high-impact (according to our field team survey) and problems for which an engineering R&D team could move the needle.
After our vetting process, a handful of customer problems rose to the top.
Exploring the Problem Space
Causality mapping helped us see the big picture and make connections.

Root cause analysis

With help from the field, we took our top problems and explored root causes. This helped us see relationships between problems and identify big pain points that lead to a lot of down-stream problems.

Down-stream effects analysis

We explored how problems often cascade into dysfunction, higher costs and frustration.

What’s interesting is we found ourselves folding problem statements from our research, which helped us see connections we did see before.

Articulating the Vision
We set out to make our vision of the user experience tangible with scenario writing.
On Thursday, we took our top ideas and wrote scenarios describing a future in which our customers are enjoying the value of our not-yet-existent products.

The idea was to take our abstract ideas and make them tangible. We were envisioning presenting a storyboard to the advisory panel on Friday and wanted our ideas to be approachable and expressive.

Scenario writing session.
Not every design process worked.
“What is the point of scenario writing? Have we learned anything?”, asked an engineer on Friday morning.

I probed the room. “What do we think?”, I asked.

There was a pattern: Even after getting feedback from colleagues who stopped by the studio space, we didn’t learn anything from the scenario writing that we didn’t already know. Once we started talking about it, it became obvious.

I’ve been here before. Not every design process works as planned.

We improvised.
The likely problem with the scenarios is that we were too far from the ground for them to work. We need something one level up in abstraction.

We had four key ideas we wanted to present to the advisory panel. Our goal was to make our ideas tangible to a diverse audience, tie concepts to data, and show different actors and how they relate to one another.

Part 3
Design Sprint
Moving in on an MVP
Enterprises manage tens of thousands of APIs. It’s painful and challenging. We started there.
We decided to first focus on offering developer-created tools (a.k.a., APIs) through a marketplace-like experience. This was part of our solution to enable independently-managed tools, which had risen to the top during our ideation week.

It’s a tough problem space. Our customer can have up to 80,000 APIs. These APIs are a tangled mess of overlapping functionality and varying levels of quality. On top of that, there are established competitors.

“If software is eating the world, it ate banking in the nineties.”
Proto-journey Mapping
Now that we were focusing on APIs, we needed a journey map. By Wednesday.
Under tight time constraints, I facilitated a proto-journey mapping workshop with four field engineers who work onsite for companies in different industries. We brainstormed user tasks in finding APIs, rolled those up into goals and folded in pain points and other context.
Field engineers helped us map out how app devs find APIs within their organizations.
Our proto-journey map defined four phases: find, analyze, test and wire (F.A.T. Wire). Each phase had goals and pain points. This model of the journey would be the template we would use to reimagine the API discovery experience.

Key drivers for app devs looking for an API:

App devs want to find existing functionality through APIs that meets their needs so they don’t have to create it themselves.
