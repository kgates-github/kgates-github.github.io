User-Centered Product Generation
A Big Ask
In 2019, I came out to London to hire designers for Pivotal’s R&D organization. Shortley after I arrived, I met with the director of product, who posed a challenge: She wanted me to discover the top problems enterprise engineers have in getting the tools they need to build software. After that, I was to organize a product team and come up with solutions.
It was a big ask, but also a big opportunity. On one hand, having a product director insist that design thinking drive product process was great. On the other hand, I was the only designer in town, at least for a while.
Our design studio space in the middle of our week-long ideation sprint.
The Challenge
Entering the World of Red Tape and Shadow IT...
Pivotal's customers are Fortune-500 companies who each employ thousands of engineers, all of whom need a diverse set of tools, such as databases and APIs to do their jobs. Getting access to these tools on cloud platforms is rife with bottlenecks, red tape and shadow IT.
Our key goals:
  • Go wide: We wanted to thoroughly understand the customer perspective in getting / granting access to software-building tools. That would mean understanding their pains, goals and any context related to their experiences.
  • Anchor everything to customers: We wanted to have an evidence-driven approach to ideation. Understanding customers would always precede solution ideas.
  • Be transdiscliplinary: Have everyone play the role of designer, but encourage alternate perspectives. Diversity brings clarity.
My Role
As design lead, I drove the research and synthesis over a period of several weeks. After that, I facilitated a week-long ideation workshop and design sprint.
Collaborators
For the research, I paired with an engineer (and aspiring designer) on a daily basis for a couple months. I also facilitated various design workshops that included engineers, PMs, stakeholders, marketing, and others.
Design Roadmapping
Applying Design Thinking to the Design Process
We prioritized design work by first understanding the problems we faced. I facilitated a workshop in which engineers, PMs and design formed open questions around our central theme of developer tool access. We grouped the questions into themes, dot voted, and discussed. We later brainstormed activities and prioritized them.
My design roadmapping workshops start with a dump-and-sort in which the whole team collectively agrees on top problems. (The image above is one section of our roadmap.)
Each design activity has a description, outcome, output and timeframe. (This design roadmap is from a more recent project.)
Why Create a Design Roadmap?
  • The hammer and nail problem: Designers (myself included), can be biased towards tackling problems we happen to know how to solve. Design roadmaps put problems first and solutions second. Not every problem is a nail.
  • Considering urgency: Design should meet the project where it is. If, say, the PM is anxious about an upcoming BETA release, we might give that our attention first.
  • Personal growth: Design roadmaps are a great way for designers to build personal growth into their day-to-day. They are free to suggest design activities that they have never done.

Getting Out of the Building
We started our discussion guide with questions from our design roadmapping, then brainstorming more open questions around our central inquiry. We wanted to find out things like “How do developers decide what tools they need?” and “What do they find frustrating?”
We interviewed around 30 customers, non-customers, and members of our consulting business who work on-site. The interviews were split between app devs and platform operators.

Synthesis
Show, Don’t Tell: Making Synthesis a Team Effort
Once our affinity map started taking shape, we asked for volunteers to help. In this picture, the engineer I collaborated with described affinity mapping to another engineer as “optimization through evolution”.
We rotated teammates onto the synthesis process so they could see for themselves how insights emerge from data:
  • In an engineering-centric culture like Pivotal’s, co-workers who have not worked with designers can be skeptical about our processes.
  • Having teammates rotate onto design activities builds credibility, which then builds trust in the data and paves the way for evidence-based decision making.
  • We were also doing research in a very technical domain, so engineers were in a good position to help us find patterns we might not have found ourselves.
Our affinity map organized our findings and captured the essence of the end-to-end experience.

Personas
Understanding Our Two Primary Users
App Developer
Cody
App developers build apps and other types of software.
Ninety-seven to ninety-nine percent of our customers’ developers are early in their careers. Most are focused on writing code and are mostly not able or comfortable working with cloud infrastructure.
Demand for Many Developer Tools
App devs collectively want access to a wide variety of tools and the latest hot tech. There are a number of reasons for this:
  • Role-specific needs: Developers come in many forms. A data scientist requires a very different set of tools than a mobile app developer.
  • Task-specific needs: Engineers sometimes start with specific needs related to the task at hand. For example, an aerospace engineering team creating flight control tests might need a time-series database with specified level of latency.
  • Resume value: Developers often want the latest hot tech. This is partly due to resume value, which can determine their viability in the job market.
“The problem with [developer tools] is like the problem with the word processor: Out of hundreds of features, only 10 percent are used, but everyone uses a different 10 percent.”
Platform Operator
Alana
Platform operators maintain the cloud platform. They are responsible for providing tools for developers, keeping the platform up-to-date, and troubleshooting when something goes wrong.
Even in large organizations, operator teams tend to be small, with 10 - 20 people. This contrasts with the fact that their platforms can be used by hundreds, even thousands of developers.
Reluctance to Provide Developer Tools
Operators want to offer a small, manageable set of tools that cover a wide surface area. Despite app developer demands, operators are often unwilling or unable to provide all of the tools developers want. There are a number of reasons for this:
  • Risk aversion: Cloud platforms are extremely complex, but tend to be stable as long as nothing changes. Introducing new tools means introducing change, and change brings risk of something going wrong.
  • Developing expertise: If an operations team offers a new technology on their platform, they are expected to be experts at that technology. When something goes wrong, the operations teams are expected to fix it.
  • Not enough demand: Operations teams are inherently small and they tend to be unwilling to support tools for which there is little demand.
  • Remembering getting burned: Consequences for problems with the platform can be severe. Downtime could mean lost revenue. A security breach might bring bad press and subpoenas. Audit teams can appear unannounced at any time, and if they uncover mistakes, shut things down for months.
“An audit team found unencrypted passwords on a VM and shut the operations team down for eight months.”
Key Findings
Our Primary Personas Are Fundamentally Misaligned
At the heart of everything was a fundemental conflict: While app developers want a wide variety of tools and the latest, hot technology, platform operators want to offer a small, manageable set of tools.

Workshop Prep
Making Feedback a Forcing Function
In a week-long, open-ended ideation session, it’s impossible to imagine the product direction you’ll come up with by Friday. Before ideation week began, I set up sources of feedback and embedded them into the whole process. Getting continuous feedback would help keep us sane as we navigated a vast problem space.
Feedback Sources
Panel of advisory experts
These included people from related product teams, field engineers and marketing from Europe and North America. I set up two sessions with them on Wednesday and Friday, and presented our progress.
The panel brought relevant and diverse persectives to the table.
Field engineer participation
Several field engineers volunteered their time. We included them in activities and asked them to evaluate early ideas.
Field engineers are great because they often work on-site for months at a time and can bring the customer perspective to the table.
Open doors
I invited the whole Pivotal office to stop by any time. When anyone showed up, one of us would take the lead and explain our current thinking, and how we got there.
Random visits made sure we could clearly articulate everything we were doing at any time.
Articulating concepts clearly requires thinking clearly and is refined through practice. We built that practice into the design process.
Problem Prioritization
Determining Where We Can Have the Most Impact
With many customer problems at hand, we needed to decide which ones to tackle. We used three techniques:
  • Field survey: We asked field engineers to choose the problems that, if solved, would have the highest impact on their customers. This helped us weight problems by customer impact.
  • Wheelhouse check: We voted on what problems could be tackled by an engineering-centric product team. We cared about problems outside our wheelhouse, as problems are often intertwined, but wanted to focus on where we would have the most impact.
  • Engineering gut check: Engineers estimated potential complexity for each top problem at the outset. We wanted to take into account whether any given problem might have high technical risk.

Root Cause Analysis
Seeing the Big Picture
Our causal map starts with a customer problem at the left (there is a lot more demand for developer toos than supply). The yellow nodes are root causes and the blue nodes are possible solutions.
Five Whys
With help from the field, we took our top problems and explored root causes by asking ourselves "why?" as many times as possible. This helped us see relationships between problems and identify big pain points that lead to a lot of down-stream problems. It also exanded the field of potential problems we could solve.
One branch of root causes.
Down-Stream Effects
We explored how problems often cascade into dysfunction, higher costs and frustration by asking ourselves "so what?" It is in these downstream effects that we can better understand what value we might be bringing to our customers.
Down-stream effects show cascade of customer problems.
Linking Solutions to Value
I wanted the team to intuitively link a given problem we might solve, say "Operations are reluctant to offer technology that requires expertise they don’t have", to cascading effects, such as "inefficiency" and "non-compliance with regulations".
Once we started ideating, the team should be able to chain solutions, such as "show average costs from other customers" to values, like "reduced compliance risk".

Scenario Writing
Tieing the Vision Together
Scenario writing session
On Thursday, we took our top ideas and wrote scenarios describing a future in which our customers are enjoying the value of our not-yet-existent products.
The idea was to take our abstract ideas and make them tangible. We were envisioning presenting a storyboard to the advisory panel on Friday and wanted our ideas to be approachable and expressive.
A Misfire and Redress
“What is the point of scenario writing? Have we learned anything?”, asked an engineer on Friday morning. I probed the room. “What do we think?”, I asked.
There was a pattern: Even after getting feedback from colleagues who stopped by the studio space, we didn’t learn anything from the scenario writing that we didn’t already know. Once we started talking about it, it became obvious.
Our scenario writing didn't work out as planned. We would need to improvise.
The likely problem with the scenarios is that we were too far from the ground for them to work. We need something one level up in abstraction.
We came up with idea boards. Idea boards conveyed concepts and tied together outcomes, data and personas.

Design Sprint
Design Sprint
Delegating the Curation and Management of Dev Tools
We decided to first focus on enabling operators to deligate responsibilities. In particular, developer-created tools (a.k.a., APIs) through a marketplace-like experience. This resonated with data we learned in our research: Enterprises manage tens of thousands of APIs, which is painful and challenging.
Our customers can have up to 80,000 APIs. These APIs are a tangled mess of overlapping functionality and varying levels of quality.

Competitive Analysis
Putting Our Rival’s Strengths and Weaknesses in Context
Two engineers on the team performed a competitive analysis of two rivals. We took this and mapped it onto our journey: A green check showed competitor strength, a red “x” weakness / opportunity, and a question mark a mixed bag. This mapping helped us visually see where we need to have parity and where there might be opportunities to differentiate.
A digitized version of our proto journey map. The checks and x's in the center rows show where competitors have strengths and weaknesses.
Potential strategic advantage
In the process of mapping our journey and competitive analysis, a potential advantage appeared: Security. Credentials are often sent through email, posing significant security risks. We effectively “own” the network on our platform and know user access rights. We could grant API access without credentials being passed around.

Reimagining the Journey
Gallery walk. Pictured are product engineers and field engineers.
Now that we understood something about the developer user journey around getting access to APIs, we set out to reimagine it. We set up a design studio in which everyone would sketch concepts that would seed our initial designs.

Finding Inspiration Outside Our Domain
Everyone had a homework assignment: Find designs in different domains that do well what we want to do (find, analyse, test, wire) and present what they like and why. This exercise gets participants to think laterally. There were a couple highlights:
Amazon.com
Presenters pointed out name bands are important in evaluating quality when making a purchase decision on Amazon. This resonated with our research: Certain engineering teams who create APIs have reputations for creating high-quality code.
downhill_skiing
Ski demo day
These days, skiers can test out new skis during demo days at ski resorts. How is this relevant to APIs? The presenter explained there is no substitute for seeing how skis actually perform in different conditions like powder and groomed runs. Might the user “test drive” APIs?

Up-Voted Ideas
The value in ideation workshops is not in the sketches or other artifacts produced, but in the conversations about them. After everyone presented ideas, we dot voted, then discussed what we liked about the top ideas.
Two-part search query
The most up-voted idea was a search field that prompts the user to complete the sentence, "I have..." In this case, a person. Once that is entered, the next field "I need..." is auto-populated with choices. This resonated with the engineers, who pointed out this is exactly how you think when looking for an API.
Machine-Learning search
The two-part search query was tied to a network database that learns over time how to best associate the "haves" and "needs".
Usage metrics
Another idea showed usage data so the user can see if the API is in use. This resonated especially with the field engineers who explained that there tends to be a lot of "dead wood" when searching for APIs.
In-browser querries
This concept conveyed an easy way to test drive the API by querying it from the browser.
