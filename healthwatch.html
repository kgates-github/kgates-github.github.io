About
A Fool's Errand
When the HealthWatch project kicked off in 2017, it was just a product manager and me.
Senior management had tasked us with coming up with an out-of-the-box data dashboard for monitoring Pivotal Cloud Foundry, an operating system for cloud platforms like Amazon’s AWS.
Neither of us thought it was a viable concept. At least on the surface. During an early stakeholder interview, a marketing director let us know that our product was a "fool’s errand”.
HealthWatch unveiled by Pivotal’s VP of R&D at the 2017 Spring One Conference
HealthWatch would go on to gain 100% traction with 30+ Fortune 500 companies we tracked.
Fast forward eight months. HealthWatch was unveiled in December 2017. Despite our initial misgivings, we found a direction that worked. Below are some key attributes and features:
Baked-in best practices
All of our designs were informed by domain experts with deep expertise. Platform operators can immediately get access to dashboards that embody best practices without having to configure anything.

API
We took an API-first approach. All of the functionality we defined, including automated testing, is available through an API. We assumed that once operators are comfortable monitoring the platform, they’ll graduate and create their own dashboards.
The path getting to a viable product was not easy. It involved a highly iterative design process. Low fidelity, co-creation and continuous feedback were part of our daily lives. There are many iterations and many layers of experience belying the design.
This is how we did it.
The Challenge
An Unvalidated Concept
Our assignment was to build an out-of-the-box platform monitoring tool, but we didn’t yet have a clear understanding of the customer problems it would address. On top of that, the market was crowded with entrenched players, many of whom were dedicated to the domain.
My Role
I drove the overall design process for about a year. I shared research responsibilities with the PM, but led co-creation, rapid prototyping, usability testing and validation.
Collaborators
I worked closely with the PM and engineers at first, as I was the lone designer for the first several months. Later on, two designers joined the project on temporary rotations. By enlarge, I worked remotely from San Francisco, while the rest of the team was based in Denver.
Design Roadmapping
Plotting a Course When There is No Clear Direction
At the onset, we had a lot of questions and not a lot of time. I was meeting with the PM everyday, and during our discussions we started capturing questions we were asking ourselves.
Our questions ranged from, “Is the product viable?” to, “Should we base our product on an open source solution?” We then started brainstorming design process ideas that might help answer those questions.
We had big questions like “Is the product viable?” and, “Should we base our product on an open source solution?”
This was the beginning of what I would later call a design roadmap. The roadmap helped us agree on priorities and map them out on a calendar, which helped us manage stakeholder expectations.

Risk Assessment
Figuring Out What to Be Worried About
The PM and I conducted an assumptions and risks exercise among ourselves and agreed that it wasn’t clear how we would fit into a crowded market. Most of our customers were already using our metrics API and had monitoring solutions.
We both agreed on our top worry: We would have to displace entrenched products to be successful.
Would we be able to find a niche
in a crowded marketplace?
We set up interviews with around 30 stakeholders, customers, and field engineers (who work onsite at our customers’ offices). Our goal was to better understand our customers’ needs with platform monitoring and if there might be a place for us.
Interviews
Wandering Into an Urgent Problem
We weren’t more than a week into interviewing when we realized there was an urgent problem: When customers upgraded the platform, their monitoring dashboards broke. That’s because when Pivotal’s component teams released new versions, they would change metric endpoints without documenting the changes.
It was painful for our users: Upgrades are the worst possible time to not have insight into the platform’s health.
We quickly found an urgent problem: When customers upgraded the platform, their monitoring dashboards broke.
This was urgent, so we didn’t delay. Two engineers joined the team around this time and the PM set them to task. It was not too hard a problem to fix (a set of tests built into Pivotal’s build process); it had just gone unnoticed until then.
Key Findings
Seeing the Customer's Perspective
What was clear was that customers want guidance. They want us to tell them how to monitor the platform.
Metrics are “esoteric”
For the uninitiated, it is difficult to know what to do with the platform’s metrics and they can be hard to interpret. Customers “want a decoder ring” to help them.
Data overkill
There are "thousands of metrics” and “customers don’t know what to monitor”. Only a relatively small number of metrics are really needed to be effective, the trick is to know which ones.
3rd-party tools are prevalant
Most customers are already paying for licenses for third-party tools and plan to use them for platform monitoring. But a “bridge” solution could be useful while they are getting up to speed with the platform.
Many teams with many needs
There are a lot of teams monitoring, all of which have different needs. Monitoring is “always a snowflake”. That meant we would need to be flexible.
“How do we even monitor this thing in the first place? What are the key metrics?”
Our affinity map grouped findings into themes, such as “product-market fit” and “learning process”.
Strategy
We Adopted a Three-Pronged Approach
Define KPIs
A lot of monitoring know-how was in the heads of our field and R&D engineers. The PM consolidated and documented this know-how in the form of KPIs.
Design a GUI
We used the KPIs as a basis for a UI that would be exemplar of how to visualize and organize metrics. This ended up being a two-way street as some of our designs defined KPIs.
Provide an API
We knew that our customers will eventually create custom solutions, so we made our metrics available by API.
Around this time, we started thinking about how we might fit in the market: Advanced users will just use the API, but those new to the platform might benefit from an opinionated GUI that would be a bridge for them as they got up and running.
This diagram conveys how when users gain experience, they’ll move over to using the API.
Our target users are platform operators who are new to monitoring. Despite that their teams tend to be small, with four to ten members, there can be more than a thousand developers using the platform once it is at scale.
Key user goals:
  • Learn how to monitor CloudFoundry
  • Understand health and operability of platform
  • Little or no customer interruptions
  • Successfully fix problems when they occur
“The last thing we want is the end user telling us about problems with our applications.”

Thinking Outside of the Screen
In a previous life in the business intelligence world, I learned an interesting perspective on data dashboard design: Dashboards don’t tell the whole story.
Instead, when a user looks at a data dashboard, they look for any new information, which they then use to update their mental model of the whole system. After that, they make an assessment of the system based on this new state.
A big part of the user experience for this product exists in the user's head, not on screens. Screens are a type of touchpoint.
Putting it All Together
The essence of the platform troubleshooting user experience goes something like this:
  • When there is a potential issue with Cloud Foundry, our users go on a journey through a constellation of alerts, dashboards, and Slack conversations.
  • They pick up bits of information from one source, make a decision, then go on to another to get the next thing they are looking for all-the-while continuously updating their mental model of the overall system.
  • Eventually, like a doctor considering a patient’s symptoms, they form a hypothesis about the actual problem and formulate possible solutions.
Collaboration
Imbuing Design with Expertise
Not too long into the design process, we realized that we faced a seemingly insurmountable task: We needed to become experts in a complex domain in order to help users become experts in the domain.
We needed to become experts in a complex domain in order to help users become experts in the domain.
Building Our Own Education Into the Design Process
To solve the problem of needing domain knowledge, we made platform monitoring experts part of design. To do this, we reached out through our networks and found coworkers with deep knowledge of platform monitoring. The idea was to create a panel of experts and have them fully embedded in the design process.
A session with the expert advisory panel.
This is how it worked: I committed to presenting to the panel whatever I was working on, in whatever state it was in, every Friday morning. We made feedback a forcing function. We didn’t invite criticism; we imposed it.
We made feedback a forcing function. We didn’t invite criticism; we imposed it.
It worked. The panel unceremoniously shot down anything that didn’t pass muster. But at the same time, they were also very engaged and shared their expertise freely. They exerted a kind of benign ruthlessness. They cared.
Co-Creating
What’s the best way to design a feature in a complex domain? Find domain experts and have them design it.
Here is an example of how we used co-creation to inform designs: First, we had a Cloud Foundry operator walk us through a time when they had to troubleshoot a problem with their system.
After they told us their story, we read through the metrics we were considering including in the dashboard to make sure everyone understood them. Then we went to a whiteboard and sketched together.
A co-creation session with an operations engineer. We took notes and annotated photos of the designs.
The idea was to co-design the dashboard the operator wished they had. We recorded the sessions and annotated photos of the white board. We then used them as a guide when we got back to our desks. The real value was not in the drawings, but in the conversations we had at the whiteboard.
The idea was to co-design the dashboard the operator wished they had.
Here are a couple of interesting things we learned:
The question “When?” is paramount
“When did the system first start to have problems?” is one of the first things operators want to know. The alert that compelled them into action might have been triggered long after the root of the problem started. We made timestamps as precise as possible.
Component IDs and timestamps are keys
Operators bounce between our app and others, such as log search tools. Component IDs and timestamps are the two things you need when jumping between systems. We add copy-to-clipboard buttons to everything we could.
This sketch shows how metrics work in concert when investigating the performance of a database: CPU usage plus throughput helps explain the nature of the issue.
Rapid Prototyping
Failing Forward—Cheaply and Constantly
Failure is good. What's important in design is learning, not being right. Rapid prototyping means being wrong constantly until you gain confidence that you aren't.
In our situaltion, it was almost impossible to thoroughly understand metrics and all their implications up front. We needed to just get ideas out of our heads and into the world quickly and start learning.
Here's an example of how rapid prototyping worked with continuous feedback from the expert advisory panel:
The evolution of our router health panel. (Last panel by Justin Roozeboom.)
The first design above on the left was our initial attempt at a router health design. It did not go over well. During our first feedback session, one panel member's first comment was, "I don't like anything about this."
R.I.P. router panel design #1.
Born: March 15, 2017. Died: March 16, 2017.
We quickly learned what we got wrong: We focused too much on router CPU and tried to force everything into a stoplight visualization.
Operators want to first understand how much traffic is flowing through the routers and whether there are any trends up or down. They then want to get an idea of how responsive requests are.
So we went back to the drawingboard. (Literally.)
R.I.P. design #2.
Our next sketches took these insights into account. The throughput chart at top went over well, as did the CPU usage at the bottom. The middle charts tried to show distributions of latency and CPU, but generally confused everyone.
R.I.P. design #3.
Next we learned that if there is a change in throughput, operators would want to know if there is a change in the number of errors, which tend to have a steady-state rate at scale. We added a chart showing errors at thee bottom. We still clung to trying to show CPU as a distribution, but it did not pass muster.
Layout and Hierarchy
For data dashboards, like in typography, meaning drives layout. Dashboards are in some ways like hieroglyphs—meaning is often created by combining several symbols, or graphs, together.
Through our iterative design process, we first understood the implications of metrics and how they naturally hung together with others. That drove layout decisions. For example, some indicators, like HTTP throughput, are key to interpreting others, and therefore are given prominence.
When there is a drop in traffic (top arrows), and an increase in errors (bottom arrows), that would mean there is likely a problem, and not a natural decrease in traffic.
When there is an increase in traffic (top arrows), and router CPU usage and latency look good, that means the platform is successfully handling the increased load.
Getting Into a Weekly Routine of Rapid Prototyping With Continuous Feedback
Every Monday, I ran an engineering review with our developers and the PM, then during the week, co-created and iterated on design directions in low fidelity.
On Fridays, I presented wireframes and sharpie sketches to the panel. Sometimes we got things right straight away; sometimes we were wrong and needed a lot of iterations. One thing we learned early on was that even a poorly drawn, half-baked design could inspire a highly-fruitful conversation.
Evolution of a design from design studio to production code. (First sketches by the PM, engineering lead and me. Last panel by Justin Roozeboom.)
Dogfooding
Making First Contact with Reality
Some things can only be learned through functioning code. HealthWatch would need to help operators navigate through unexpected disruptions, the nature of which can't be predicted. We needed to test with real data.
Pivotal’s cloud operations team in Ireland agreed to be the first to use HealthWatch in a production environment. That team hosted a SaaS product called Pivotal Tracker, an agile project management tool which has several hundred-thousand active users.
We conducted several activities to garner feedback.
HealthWatch on a wall-mounted display in Pivot’s Dublin office. Operators should be able to tell if the platform is healthy from 20 to 30 feet away.
Weekly Checkins
We conducted weekly check in session with the cloud ops team in Dublin. In these sessions, they walked us through scenarios where they used HealthWatch. We got a lot of usability feedback, but also validated that the app helped them get through unexpected problems, such as failed upgrades.
Closed Card Sort
Once the operations team had been using HealthWatch for several weeks, we gave operations team members workbooks in Google Draw that contained movable metrics panels. We then asked them to arrange the dashboard the way they would like it.
Alpha testers arranged their ideal dashboard layout.
After that, we interviewed the operators to understand the thinking behind their layouts. This eventually led us to grouping metrics into three areas of impact:
End-user Impact
End users affected. Generally urgent.
Developer Impact
Not as urgent as end-user impact, but potentially important.
Platform Impact
Underlying VMs and other platform infrastructure.
Dot Voting
After the card sort, we had team members independently tell us what metrics they would remove from the main dashboard and why.
This got us to declutter and move about 25 percent of the main dashboard to deep dive views.

Conclusion
Going to Market
Bringing HealthWatch to market wasn’t easy. The design process had many ups and downs, but we got through it.
Measurable Outcomes
Measurable outcomes in the enterprise world are tricky. Measurable outcomes should be leading indicators that keep everyone honest, but in the enterprise, uptake of products is slow and indicators tend to be lagging — Pivotal's sales cycles are about 18 months.
We came up with a leading indicator: Five given customers will install our closed beta and give us three one-hour feedback sessions. This tests whether customers are willing to "pay" for the product. It also builds in customer feedback.

Learnings
The expert advisory panel worked well
By meet regularly, the panel felt they were part of the process. We developed a repore, which meant we got comfortable getting critical feedback. In addition, the field engineers on the panel were, in the end, basically SMEs (subject matter experts). They trained other field engineers.
Dogfooding had a lot of benefits
The feedback was great, but dogfooding also had a marketing angle: We could say, “We use this product ourselves.”
Emergent design came with challenges
The design we came up with was not top-down. It emerged over several weeks. This was good in a lot of ways, but had some drawbacks. IA was challenging. We made design decisions optimized locally and when stepped back, getting everything to sync was difficult.
We got UCD and Agile to play fairly nice
A big challenge for me at the time of this project was figuring out how to make UCD work with Agile. Pivotal's R&D department was almost cult-like in it's adherence to Agile processes and UCD was a pretty new concept for most people.
Our weekly design cadence with regular interaction with engineering worked. A lot of this is due to the fact that our design was emergent. There is still work to be done in figuring our how to sync the oscillating cadence of a UCD process with the relentless momentum of an Agile dev team.
