About
A Fool's Errand

When the HealthWatch project kicked off in 2017, it was just a product manager and me.

Senior management had tasked us with coming up with an out-of-the-box data dashboard for monitoring Pivotal Cloud Foundry, an operating system for cloud platforms like Amazon’s AWS.

Neither of us thought it was a viable concept. At least on the surface. During an early stake holder interview, a marketing director let us know that our product was “a fool’s errand”.

The Challenge
An Unvalidated Concept
Our assignment was to build an out-of-the-box platform monitoring tool, but we didn’t yet have a clear understanding of the customer problems it would address. On top of that, the market was crowded with entrenched players, many of whom were dedicated to the domain.
My Role
I drove the overall design process for about a year. I shared reseach responsibilites with the PM, but led co-creation, rapid prototyping, usability testing and validation.
Collaborators
I worked closely with the PM and engineers at first, as I was the lone designer first several months. Later on, two other designers joined the project on temporary rotations. By enlarge, I worked remotely from San Francisco, while the rest of the team was based in Denver.
HealthWatch unvieled by Pivotal’s VP of R&D at the 2017 Spring One Conference
HealthWatch would go on to gain 100% traction with 30+ Fortune 500 companies we tracked.

arrow_forward
Fast forward eight months. HealthWatch was unvieled in December 2017. Despite our initial missgivings, we had found a direction that worked.
The path getting there was not easy. It involved a highly iterative design process. Low fidelity, co-creation and continuous feedback were part of our daily lives. There are many iterations and many layers of experience belying the design.
This is how we did it.
Design Roadmapping
At the onset, we had a lot of questions and not a lot of time.
I was meeting with the PM everyday and during our discussions, we started capturing questions we were asking ourselves. They ranged from, “Is the product viable?” to, “Should we base our product on an open source solution?” We then started brainstorming design process ideas that might help answer those questions.
This was the beginings of what I would later call a design roadmap. The roadmap helped us agree on prioities and map them out on a calendar, which helped us manage stakeholder expectations.

Risk Assessment
Our top worry: We would have to displace entrenched products to be successful.
The PM and I conducted an assumtions and risks exercise among ourselves and agreed that it wasn’t clear how we would fit into a crowded market. Most of our customers were already using our metrics API and had monitoring solutions.
Would we be able to find a niche
in a crowded marketplace?
We set up interviews with around 30 stakeholders, customers, and field engineers (who work onsite at our customers’ offices). Our goal was to better understand our customers’ needs with platform monitoring and if there might be a place for us.
Interviews
We happened upon a trainwreck. Thankfully.
We weren’t more than two weeks into interviewing when a clear pattern emerged: When customers upgraded the platform, their monitoring dashboards broke. That’s because when Pivotal’s component teams released new versions, they would change metric endpoints without documenting the changes.
It was painful for our users: Upgrades are the worst possible time to not have insight into the platform’s health.
This seemed urgent, so we didn’t delay. Two engineers joined the team around this time and the PM set them to task. It was not too hard a problem to fix (a set of tests built into Pivotal’s build process); it had just gone unnoticed until then.
“When [the customer] upgraded from 1.5 to 1.6, it was a trainwreck. There was remediation.”
Key Findings
Customers want guidance. They want us to tell them how to monitor the platform.
Metrics are “esoteric”
For the uninitiated, it is difficult to know what to do with the platform’s metrics and they can be hard to interpret. Customers “want a decoder ring” to help them.
Data overkill
There are "thousands of metrics” and “customers don’t know what to monitor”. Only a reletively small number of metrics are really needed to be effective, the trick is to know which ones.
3rd-party tools are prevelant
Most customers are already paying for licenses for third-party tools and plan to use them for platform monitoring. But a “bridge” solution could be useful while they are getting up to speed with the platform.
Many teams with many needs
There are a lot of teams monitoring, all of which have different needs. Monitoring is “always a snowflake”. That meant we would need to be flexible.
“How do we even monitor this thing in the first place? What are the key metrics?”
Our affinity map grouped findings into themes,

Strategy
We adopted a three-pronged strategy
Define KPIs
A lot of monitoring know-how was in the heads of our field and R&D engineers. The PM consolidated and documented this know-how in the form of KPIs.
Design a GUI
We used the KPIs as a basis for a UI that would be exemplar of how to visualize and organize metrics. This ended up being a two-way street as some of our designs defined KPIs.
Provide an API
We knew that our customers will eventually create custom solutions, so we made our metrics available by API.
Around this time, we started thinking about how we might fit in the market: Advanced users will just use the API, but those new to the platform might benefit from an opinionated GUI that would be a bridge for them as they got up and running.
This diagram conveys how when users gain experience, they’ll move over to using the API.
Our target users are platform operators who are new to monitoring. Despite that their teams tend to be small, with four to ten members, there can be more than a thousand developers using the platform once it is at scale.
Key user goals:
  • Learn how to monitor CloudFoundry
  • Understand health and operability of platform
  • Little or no customer interuptions
  • Successfully fix problems when they occur
“The last thing we want is the end user telling us about problems with our applications.”
Journeymapping
Thinking outside of the screen
In a previous life in the business intellignece world, I learned an interesting perspective on data dashboard design: Dashboards don’t tell the whole story.
Instead, when a user looks at a data dashboard, they look for new information and use it to update their mental model of the whole system. They then make an assessment of the system based this new state. The essence of the user experience is all in the head.
A non-linear user experience
Around this time, I paired up with an operations engineer at Pivotal and together we created an outline of their monitoring workflow. Based on this and data from out research track, I created a journey map. This map captured the non-linear nature of the user experience, and how our product would be part of a larger ecosystem with many intertwined components.
Our journey map captured the non-linear nature of the user experience. It also conveyed that we were part of a larger ecosystem.
Putting it all together
The essence of the user experience goes something like this: When there is a potential issue with Cloud Foundry, our users go on a journey through an constellation of alerts, dashboards, and Slack conversations. They pick up bits of information from one source, make a decision, then go on to another to get the next thing they are looking for, all-the-while continuously updating their mental model of the overall system.
Eventually, like a doctor considering a patient’s symptoms, they form a hypothosis about the actual problem and formulate possible solutions.
Rapid Prototyping
We made learning part of the design process.
When we started designing, we faced what seemed like an insurmountable task: We needed to become experts in a complex domain in order help users become experts in the domain.
On Cloud Foundry, a down VM might be reason to panic. Or it might not mean anything. It can depend on a lot of factors. What else is going on? Is there an update to the system in progress? If that’s the case, it’s normal for certain VMs to go down temporarily. On the other hand, some VMs are mission-critical, but only under certain circumstances. There is no one thing or easy answer.
We needed to become experts in a complex domain in order help users become experts in the domain.
The pressure was on. Engineers were wrapping up their current work and would soon start writing code for HealthWatch, which didn’t yet have clear form.
We embedded platform monitoring experts in the design process.
To do this, we reached out through our networks and found coworkers with deep knowledge of platform monitoring. The idea was to create a panel of experts and have them fully embedded in the design process.
A session with the expert advisory panel.
This is how it worked: I committed to presenting to the panel whatever I was working on, in whatever state it was in, every Friday morning. There was to be no escape from the experts. We didn’t invite criticism. We imposed it.
It worked. The panel unceremoniously shot down anything that didn’t pass muster. But at the same time, they were also very engaged and shared their expertise freely. They exerted a kind of benign ruthlessness. They cared.
Expert advisory panel feedback
Here's an example of how the expert advisory panel affected our approach to designing for router health metrics:
The evolution of our router health panel. (Last panel by Justin Roozeboom.)
The first panel above was our initial attempt at a router health design. It did not go over well. One of the panel members said, "I dont' like anything about this."
We quickly learned what we got wrong: We focused too much on router CPU and tried to force everything into a stoplight visualization.
Operators want to first understand how much traffic is flowing through the routers and whether there are any trends up or down. If there is a potential problem, operators would want to know if there is a change in the number of errors, which tend to have a steady-state rate at scale. After that, CPU usage becomes relevant.
Our sketches and wireframes took these insights into account, and we eventually got to the final design, on the right.

Here are a couple of interesting things we learned:
The question “When?” is paramount
“When did the system first start to have problems?” is one of the first things operators want to know. The alert that complelled them into action might have been triggered long after the root of the problem started. We made timestamps as precise as possible.
Component IDs and timestamps are keys
Operators bounce between our app and others, such as log search tools. Component IDs and timestamps are the two things you need when jumping between systems. We add copy-to-clipboard buttons to everything we could.
This sketch shows how metrics work in concert when investigating the performance of a database: CPU usage plus throughput helps explain the nature of the issue.
As the project took off, we got into a weekly routine of rapid prototyping with continuous feedback.
Every Monday, I ran a design review with engineering and the PM, then during the week, co-created and iterated on design directions in low fidelity.
On Fridays, I presented wireframes and sharpie sketches to the panel. Sometimes we got things right straight away; sometimes we were wrong and needed a lot of iterations. One thing we learned early on was that even a poorly drawn, half-baked design could inspire a highly-fruitful conversation.
Emergent Design
Designs emerged as we iterated and learned.
Take the CLI command line health panel. It answers the question, “Can developers get their code up on the platform?” and much more.
The CLI Command Health panel tells operators whether developers can get their code onto the platform. There is a lot more to it than the UI. (Visual design by Justin Roozeboom.)
The way it works is every five minutes, an automated process pushes a tiny app onto the platform. HealthWatch then tests it so see if the app is healthy and shows the results in the form of stoplights that are either green, yellow or red.
It was simple by design, but it took a few iterations to to finesse the UI and underlying logic.
The sketches above are from a design studio I ran with the PM, engineers and myself. Our heads seemed to be in the same place.
The expert panel liked the version that showed history: When a user gets back from lunch, and something is failing, they will want to know how long there has been a problem.
The design evolved as we got feedback. We added a new stoplight, debated hierachy and streamlined the look and feel. (Last panel by Justin Roozeboom)
The design was not just UI work. We also defined the underlying logic.
In order to define the stoplight logic, we had to take a number of things into account:
A failed test isn’t necessarily an emergency
Cloud Foundry is self-correcting. A failed test might be the result of an upgrade or some other temporary cause. The trick is you want to show a red stop light when there is a real problem and not a false alarm.
Tests are interdependent
For example, if the “start app” test fails, you can’t see if it is emitting logs because the app doen’t exist.
Several tests run concurrently
HealthWatch runs three test simutaneously. We had to interpret sequential failures versus concurent failures. We were concerned that since random test failures (one or two per hour) might be over represented with concurency.
Dogfooding
Some things can only be learned through funtioning code.
HealthWatch on a wall-mounted display in Pivot’s Dublin office. Operators should be able to tell if the platform is healthy from 20 to 30 feet away.
Pivotal’s cloud operations team in Ireland agreed to be the first to use HealthWatch in a production environment. That team hosted a SaaS product called Pivotal Tracker, an agile project management tool which has several hundred-thousand active users.
We conducted several activities to garner feedback.
Dot voting
Once the operations team had been using HealthWatch for several weeks, we had four team member independently tell us what metrics they would remove from the main dashboard and why.
This got us to declutter and move about 25 percent of the main dashboard to deep dive views.
Alpha testers voted metrics off the island.
Card sorting
After dot voting, we gave operations team members workbooks in the Google Draw app that contained movable metrics panels. We then asked them to arrange the dashboard the way they would like it.
Alpha testers arranged their ideal dashboard layout.
After that, we interviewed the operators to understand the thinking behind their layouts. This eventually led us to gouping metrics into three areas of impact:
End-user Impact
End users affected. Generally urgent.
Developer Impact
Not as urgent as end-user impact, but potentially important.
Platform Impact
Underlying VMs and other platform infrastructure.

Learnings
The expert advisory panel worked really well
I went on use a similar practice on several other projects. A lot of things worked. By meet regularly, the panel felt they were part of the process. We developed a repore, which meant we got comforatable getting critical feedback. In addition, the field engineers on the panel were, in the end, basically SMEs (subject matter experts). They trained other field engineers.
Dogfooding had a lot of benefits
The feedback was great, but dogfooding also had a marketing angle: we could say, “We use this product ourselves.”
Emergent design came with challenges
The design we came up with was not top-down. It emerged over several weeks. This was good in a lot of ways, but had some drawbacks. IA was challenging. We made design decisions optimized locally and when stepped back, getting everything to sync was difficult.
