A Fool's Errand

When the HealthWatch project kicked off in 2017, it was just a product manager and me.

Senior management had tasked us with coming up with an out-of-the-box data dashboard for monitoring Pivotal Cloud Foundry, an operating system for cloud platforms like Amazon’s AWS.

Neither of us thought it was a viable concept. At least on the surface. During an early stake holder interview, a marketing director let us know that our product was “a fool’s errand”.

The Challenge
An Unvalidated Concept
Our assignment was to build an out-of-the-box platform monitoring tool, but we didn’t yet have a clear understanding of the customer problems it would address. On top of that, the market was crowded with entrenched players, many of whom were dedicated to the domain.
My Role
I drove the overall design process for about a year. I shared reseach responsibilites with the PM, but led co-creation, rapid prototyping, usability testing and validation.
Collaborators
I worked closely with the PM and engineers at first, as I was the lone designer first several months. Later on, two other designers joined the project on temporary rotations.
HealthWatch unvieled by Pivotal’s VP of R&D at the 2017 Spring One Conference
HealthWatch would go on to gain 100% traction with 30+ Fortune 500 companies we tracked.
See how it works
Fast forward eight months. HealthWatch was unvieled in December 2017. Despite our initial missgivings, we had found a direction that worked.
The path getting there was not easy. It involved a highly iterative design process. Low fidelity, co-creation and continuous feedback were part of our daily lives. There are many iterations and many layers of experience belying the design.
This is how we did it.
Design Roadmapping
At the onset, we had a lot of questions and not a lot of time.
I was meeting with the PM everyday and during our discussions, we started capturing questions we were asking ourselves. They ranged from, “Is the product viable?” to, “Should we base our product on an open source solution?” We then started brainstorming design process ideas that might help answer those questions.
This was the beginings of what I would later call a design roadmap. The roadmap helped us agree on prioities and map them out on a calendar, which helped us manage stakeholder expectations.

Risk Assessment
Our top worry: We would have to displace entrenched products to be successful.
The PM and I conducted an assumtions and risks exercise among ourselves and agreed that it wasn’t clear how we would fit into a crowded market. Most of our customers were already using our metrics API and had monitoring solutions.
Would we be able to find a niche
in a crowded marketplace?
We set up interviews with around 30 stakeholders, customers, and field engineers (who work onsite at our customers’ offices). Our goal was to better understand our customers’ needs with platform monitoring and if there might be a place for us.
Interviews
We happened upon a trainwreck. Thankfully.
We weren’t more than two weeks into interviewing when a clear pattern emerged: When customers upgraded the platform, their monitoring dashboards broke. That’s because when Pivotal’s component teams released new versions, they would change metric endpoints without documenting the changes.
It was painful for our users: Upgrades are the worst possible time to not have insight into the platform’s health.
This seemed urgent, so we didn’t delay. Two engineers joined the team around this time and the PM set them to task. It was not too hard a problem to fix (a set of tests built into Pivotal’s build process); it had just gone unnoticed until then.
“When [the customer] upgraded from 1.5 to 1.6, it was a trainwreck. There was remediation.”
Key Findings
Customers want guidance. They want us to tell them how to monitor the platform.
Metrics are “esoteric”
For the uninitiated, it is difficult to know what to do with the platform’s metrics and they can be hard to interpret. Customers “want a decoder ring” to help them.
Data overkill
There are "thousands of metrics” and “customers don’t know what to monitor”. Only a reletively small number of metrics are really needed to be effective, the trick is to know which ones.
3rd-party tools are prevelant
Most customers are already paying for licenses for third-party tools and plan to use them for platform monitoring. But a “bridge” solution could be useful while they are getting up to speed with the platform.
Many teams with many needs
There are a lot of teams monitoring, all of which have different needs. Monitoring is “always a snowflake”. That meant we would need to be flexible.
“How do we even monitor this thing in the first place? What are the key metrics?”
