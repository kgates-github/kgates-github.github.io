About
Humanizing Open Source
When a PM and I started this project, we thought it would be quick and easy.
VMWare had acquired a company that makes KNative, an open source technology that helps developers deploy apps on Kubernetes, a popular cloud-based operating system. The initial idea was to perform a heuristic evaluation of KNative’s GUI, make a few recommendations, and that would be the end of it.
What we found was a UI that needed a lot of work. There had been little or no thought given to user goals, flows or even hierarchy and layout. We had a hard time even figuring out how to apply basic usability heuristics.
We concluded that we couldn’t fix the UI half way; we would need to redesign it. Quick and easy was not to be.

The Challenge
Creating a usable proprietary GUI
Many of our customers require a graphical interface. With a CLI-based beta version deployed, we felt we were trailing our competitors in providing a GUI.
My Role
As design lead, I drove the overall approach to design. I worked closely with a PM in assessing risk, planning workshops, conducting user interviews, rapid prototyping, and creating wireframes.
Collaborators
On a daily basis, I worked with a product manager and engineering lead. The PM and I facilitated an engineering-driven ideation session, and after that, many feedback sessions. Two visual designers rotated onto the project towards the end.
Business Case
Assessing risk in the open-source world
We were building on open-source technology that had already survived contact with reality. That shaped how we thought about risk.
Demand
Demand for serverless technology is well-established and growing. In particular, demand is largely for hosted serverless solutions, which is our market.
According to the 2020 Cloud Native Computing Foundation (CNCF) survey, 30% of organizations polled have production apps running on serverless and another 35% are considering it. The CNCF "fully expect this growth to continue". In addition, 82% of organizations who are using serverless are either using hosted serverless solutions.

Wireframing
Refining a direction
Our stakeholders liked the direction of a recovery-themed map I created, which used data about the 2009 stimulus bill. We got the go-ahead to move forward.
The first wireframes showed stimulus projects color-coded by category. These categories corresponded to the campaign's core policy areas: education, energy, health and infrastructure.
Using Google's mapping API, we could detect the users location, and just show data points from their area.
The first wireframes I created show the top five recovery projects per state. Projects are color-coded to correspond to campaign themes: education, energy, health and infrastructure. The maps were created using real data.
As the user zooms in, more and more projects are revealed. At each zoom level, the most relevant projects are highlighted in the inset at the left.
People Management
Inspiring volunteers
With a green light from headquarters, momentum started building. We would need engineers and since most of the data was uncategorized, we would need volunteers to label about 2,000 projects.
The question lingered: How could we motivate volunteers to help hit deadlines?
Assume intrinsic motivation
The engineers who volunteered were willing to go through an interview process, so they likely want to help the president get re-elected.
Act like shipping product is inevitable
One thing I know from my background as a developer is that engineers are fundamentally creative and largely motivated by bringing something new into the world. If it looks like we are going to ship, engineers will be more likely to help. Momentum then builds in a virtuous cycle.
Be clear about scope
Break work down into doable chunks. Be sure volunteers know exactly what is expected of them and when things need to be done.
A work book I created to let volunteers help categorize recovery projects by interpreting unformated text. We categorized more than 2,000 projects with the help of volunteers.
Pivot
Adapting to chaos
In July, things abruptly changed for the worse. Just as the recovery map concept started to take shape, the Romney campaign attacked the stimulus bill. The Obama campaign banned the mention of it. The project was stopped in its tracks.
Campaign headquarters put the kibosh on our project. We were stopped in our tracks.
Everything could have easily ended at this point. Rather than give up, I went back and re-evaluated the data sets we hadn't used, and came up with a new solution.
The new concept detected the user's location and displayed the president's accomplishments across all of the campaign's policy areas. The digital director at headquarters liked the new direction, and gave us the green light.
Early version of our new direction. This concept pulls in relevant data in about health, education, tranportation etc.
Prototyping
Fully functional wireframes
Due to time constraints, I skipped wire framing and worked directly with the prototype we had started. Our goal was to have a fully functional app with just enough opinion about the UI. The branding team in Chicago would take it from there.
One challenge we faced was that some county-level data points didn't exist. When that was the case, we show county data when we had it, and state data when we didn't. In other words, our app's logic mande the information displayed as local as possible.
Using Twitter's Bootstrap UI framework, I was able to simultaneously design for tablets and phones, helping us keep to a very tight timeline.
An early version with all the working components. The app detects where the user is located and supplies talking points relevant to them across key policy areas.
The final version of the app after the branding team's work.
Conclusion
Launching on Obama's Twitter account
The final weeks of the campaign were total chaos. Despite this, we managed to launch Project Atlas the morning of October 1st. Later that day, the campaign sent the URL out on Obama's Twitter account, where it reached the president's 42 million followers.
