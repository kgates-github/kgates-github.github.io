About
Humanizing Open Source
When a PM and I started this project, we thought it would be quick and easy.
VMWare had acquired a company that makes KNative Serverless, an open source technology that helps developers deploy apps on Kubernetes, a popular cloud-based operating system.
Our initial idea was to perform a heuristic evaluation of KNative’s GUI, make a few recommendations, and that would be the end of it. Instead, we ended up redesigning the UI from the ground up.
The Data Model as the User Interface
When we evaluated KNative's open-source UI, we found there had been little or no thought given to user goals, flows, hierarchy, or layout. We realized pretty quickly that we couldn’t fix the UI half way.
Database tables as the UI
The main interface consisted of four tables which seemed like tables you might find in a database (configurations, status messages, routes, revisions).
Disjointed layout
Information was not laid out logically. For example, system status information relevant to the table in the lower left appeared on the upper right.
Poor UX flow
The only actions available were a bit of clickable text on the upper left that said "configuration", which led to an editable config file, and a delete button on the upper right.
What we would go on to learn in our design process is that the database-tables-as-the-UI is a good interface for a segment of users, but for others it is awful. We would discover real tension and complexity pulling from different directions.
Quick and easy was not to be.

The Challenge
Trailing the Competition
Once we realized we needed to redesign the GUI from the ground up, we decided to make make it part of out propritary offering.
We felt we were trailing competitors. IBM, Google and others were already providing KNative serverless solutions with proprietary GUIs. Large enterprises don't allow many of their developers direct access to software-building tools, which means GUIs are not a nice-to-haves, but requirements for adoption.
My Role
As design lead, I drove the overall approach to design. I worked closely with a PM in planning workshops, conducting user interviews, and rapid prototyping.
Collaborators
On a daily basis, I worked with a product manager and engineering lead. Two visual designers rotated onto the project towards the end.
Business Case
Assessing Risk in the World of Open Source
We were building on open-source technology that had already survived contact with reality. That shaped how we thought about risk.
Demand
Demand for serverless technology is well-established and growing. According to the 2020 Cloud Native Computing Foundation (CNCF) survey, 30% of organizations polled have production apps running on serverless and another 35% are considering it. The CNCF "fully expect this growth to continue". In addition, 82% of organizations who are using serverless are using hosted solutions.

Value
Open-source serverless functionality has been validated in the marketplace. It demonstrably solves well-known IT problems:
Developer efficiency: Serverless reduces complexity of pushing code to the Kubernetes-enabled cloud.
Resource efficiency: Serverless automatically frees up unused memory and CPU allocations in the cloud (“scales to zero”).
Emergency recovery: Serverless automatically creates application backups.
Traffic splitting: Serverless makes it easy to split traffic between versions for A/B testing and gradual rollouts.
Design Strategy
Targeting Users by Skill Level
For most large-scale enterprises, 97 to 99% of developers are early-career or contractors, and don’t have skills in managing and configuring cloud-based infrastructure [1].
Our design strategy took this into account: We assumed most developers will be early-career and new to serverless, but will gain skill over time.
This matrix shows how we targeted users by skill level, and who by definition should be the largest user cohort. Our goal is to get users from the left column to the middle column with little friction.
Strategic Objectives
Lower barriers to adoption to allow scale: Enable developers who are new to serverless to deploy to the technology regardless of their expertise in cloud infrastructure. This will allow customers to benefit from the value brought by serverless at scale.
We assumed most developers will be early-career and new to serverless, but will gain skill over time.
Help developers become efficient: Developers tend to gain expertise with technology over time, and will often graduate from using a GUI to more efficient workflows using the CLI and API. Our design should enable this gain in skill and confidence with little or no friction.
Get out of the way of advanced users: High-skill developers will automate as much as possible. They comprise a small percentage of enterprise IT, but can play an outsized role. Get out of their way and provide an API.
Journey Mapping
Laying Out a Goal-Directed Narrative
The PM had created story maps by analyzing the open-source functionality, which did a good job at outlining features and aligning them with outcomes.
Example of the story mapping format the PM used
The problem is they didn't capture the narrative of the experience. User experiences are a series of events that take place in the physical world in service of people achieving goals, which are often interconnected in logical ways.
Proto-Journey Map
I facilitated a workshop in which we deconstructed the story maps and created a narrative in the form of what I call a proto-journey map.
Objectives:
  • Have an agreed-upon model of what we as a team think we know about the current state of the journey.
  • Have a linear narrative which allow us to see the big picture and how tasks are connected and in service of the user reaching goals.
  • Have a way to fold in and organize other materials such as research findings and competitive analysis.
  • Have a foundation for ideation sessions in which we rethink the journey.
Anatomy of a proto-journey map. Stories were deconstructed and arranged a linear narrative with tasks, goals and milestones.

Competitive Analysis
Finding Strengths and Weaknesses
We analyzed competing products to ensure parity and identify gaps. We took screenshots of competitors' products and pasted them along our journey. This allowed us to see how Google, IBM and the OS version handled specific tasks. Our goal was to make sure we had parity with competitors and to find inspiration.
We walked through competitor's UIs, took screen shots and pasted them along our journey. Each screen has yellow and black stickies that contain our thoughts on strengths and weaknesses.
Competitor strengths
  • Google’s UI is generally well thought through and adhered to good UX heuristics.
  • We particularly liked Google’s stepper approach to creating and updating serverless apps and contextual help.
  • RedHat had advanced features we liked, such as allowing more experienced users to paste configurations into the UI rather than fill out a form.
Competitor weaknesses
  • Most UIs reflected data structures, not user mental models. Elements in UI not logically grouped together.
  • Poor / confusing wayfinding
  • System status messages incoherent
  • Lack of guided step-by-step flows or beginners (“Paste YAML here”)
Ideation
Conjuring the Doable, Desirable, and Differentiating
Now that we understood something about our competitors and we had a model of the user journey, we set out to reimagine it.
We set up a ideation workshop (a.k.a., design studio) in which everyone (engineers, field engineers, design and PM) would sketch concepts that would seed our initial designs.
My goal was to provoke a collision of ideas that come from multiple perspectives. These collisions are what I've found result in concepts that are useful, doable, desirable, and differentiating.
When running a design studio, there are a few things that are key:
Getting the right people in the room
In my experience, this means customer advocates (if not actual customers), people from related projects, engineers, and domain experts.
Getting everyone into the user's shoes
That means getting people comfortable with the process, encouraging them to think laterally and, framing the problem from the user’s perspective.
Before we started sketching, we reviewed the jouney map and personas.
Discouraging ownership of ideas
I sometimes require participants to steal ideas from others and explain what they stole and why. If two ideas work great together, that will be good for customers. Allowing ownership of ideas impedes that.
Blurring boundaries
The overall goal is to be transdisciplinary — meaning boundaries between disciplines are blurred to the point of being almost nonexistent. Great ideas arise when designers think like engineers, who think like domain experts, who think like designers, and everyone is stealing ideas from everyone else.

A Sampling of Ideas
Intuitive information layout
Several people came up with similar designs that merged information from multiple sources into intuitive tables. Many had a visual representation of status.
Guided UX Flows
This sketch depicts stepper-like functionality. One thing we liked was that it showed how users can perform one task many different ways.
Performance analysis
Serverless technology will automatically free up system resources when an app has no traffic. When traffic returns, starting up cold can be slow. Showing how long cold starts take would enable developers to optimize for more responsiveness.
Change Log
Changes to apps (revisions) are described in YAML. This feature would create a diff between revisions, allowing developers to see exactly what changed. This could be an interesting place to start debugging. Debugging is the top pain point for serverless users[1]
Rapid Prototyping
Iterating Towards Understanding
Cloud computing is very complex, and most designs I've worked on in the domain are like multivariate problems: There are many possible outcomes, which are dependent on many variables, which are often related in unknown ways.
No human is smart enough to solve problems like these by themselves, so I don't try. Instead, I make learning part of the process through collaboration and feedback.
Grooming Seed Ideas Into a Direction
First, we ran a mini sketching session with the PM, another designer and myself. The goal was to take ideas from the previous design studio and groom them into one or two possible directions.
Our inital attemps at grooming solutions show we were largely in alignment.
Evaluative and Generative Feedback
Once we had a direction, we wanted to test it with field engineers who are embedded on customer dev teams. The sessions had two parts: usability tests and generative questions. We wanted to get feedback on our design, but we also wanted to probe into some general questions we had, such as when developers use GUIs.
We created a testing plan and wrote a discussion guide. We split our tests between users familiar with serverless technology and those who weren't. We then ran a batch of usability / research sessions, updated wireframes based on what we learned, then did it again, several times.
Our usability tests had three primary user scenarios: Create, update and route traffic.
We went through many iterations in two weeks of prototyping.
We synthesized feedback in-line to maintain context
Key Findings
We over simplified
We simplified the UI too much, and strayed too far from the underlying technology. This risked introducing confusion and disharmony, especially when users toggle between the GUI and CLI.
We needed to stay true to underlying concepts and language used in Kubernetes and serverless functionality.
Novice users were confused
Users with no experience with serverless were completely confused. One tester thought he was configuring a database for the first 10 minutes of the interview.
A big part of the confusion was caused by a few terms associated with serverless technology that were overloaded (in particular, "service" and "traffic").
Default settings are really important
When a developer is setting up serverless for the first time, they will have no idea how much memory their app will need. By asking this, we were forcing a question on them that could not be answered.
GUIs can provide value for all users
GUIs can provide value for new users, early-career developers, seasoned veterans, and even expert users.
This reinforced our initial assumptions. What we didn't think of is that platform operators would benefit too. Our GUI would lower cognitive load to operator tasks like traffic splitting and troubleshooting.
“When a developer doesn’t know much about a new technology, just clicking a box is powerful.”
Refining Design
Pulling It All Together
After several iterations and feedback sessions, our wireframes started to feel more and more solid. There were several forces at work in our designs:
Business strategy: Lower barriers to adoption
Design strategy: Target users by skill level
Harmony: Stay true to KNative conventions
User experience: Use well-known UX patterns

Conclusion
Learnings
The project was unexpectedly challenging
We designed a UI on top of two very opionated APIs (KNative and Kubernetes) and those opinions were ofted at odds with good UX principles. That created tension between the UX for advanced users, who want to see concepts they are familiar with, and novice users, who we wanted to hand-hold.
Embedding competitive analysis in the journey map is a keeper
We found ourselves referring back to the analysis often, and I got feedback that others were doing the same.
An idea has been rattling around in my head for a while to turn the competitive anlysis into a full workshop. It would entail laying out competitors' UI screenshots in the journey map, then writing down strengths and weaknesses. These could be synthesized and rolled up into a kind of in-line SWOT analysis.
Engineers have a creative superpower
Engineers can imagine possibilities that are unthinkable to we lay people. These unthinkable thoughts hold the potential for differentiating features. This just reinforces what I already know; I always include engineers in the creative process : ) You can read more about that here.
