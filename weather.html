Prototyping a Mobile-First Data App
An Exploration of Data Visualization and React Native for Mobile Design
The goal of this project was to exercise skills I haven't used in a while, particularly mobile design and JavaScript.
For this project, I created a weather app that combines weather and air pollution forecasts. I wanted to explore mobile design patterns while leveraging my background in data visualization. I also wanted to learn a bit of React Native.
Screenshots of the prototype running on an iPhone SE, iPhone 13 Mini, iPhone 13 Max, and a 12.9-inch iPad Pro.
Discovery and Concept Exploration
Discovery and Concept Exploration
Analysis and Sketching
I started off by exploring publicly-available data sets: The U.S. government’s weather service API, the EPA’s climate data and OpenWeather.com. I wanted to get a sense of what was out there and what I might include in my app.
I found myself drawn to two things I generally had not seen in weather apps: historical data and air pollution forecasts. But at this point, I wasn't sure what I might do with them.
I found myself drawn to two things: historical data and air pollution forecasts, but I wasn't sure what I might do with them at first.
Emergent User Needs
Once I started sketching concepts out on paper, I realized something early on: I had a hard time reasoning about my concepts without some kind of criteria against which to evaluate feature ideas.
I started writing questions down on my drawing paper that I wanted the app to answer for users. These included things like “Will I need to bring a raincoat to work tomorrow?” and “Will it be warm enough tonight to eat dinner on the patio?”.
In early sketches, I started writing questions the app should be able to answer, such as "What's it like outside now?" and, "What is normal for this time of year?".
I realized the questions were filling the role that research findings would normally play in my regular design process. The questions also hung together in logical ways and groups of questions chained together formed something like journeys or user scenarios.
Learning Through Iteration
As I iterated on the designs, I was able to suss out trade-offs between approaches. For example, I started with a boxplot chart showing highs and lows for the week. This answered the question, "What's the week going to be like?".
Early sketch and wireframe showing forecasts as box plots.
This approach visually conveyed the week's temperature trend, but I couldn't come up with a graceful way to show hourly data (I initially included a toggle button). In addition the showing the outlook for the week, the app needed to answer more specific questions such as, "How cold will it be tomorrow morning when I walk to work?".
Sketch and wireframe exploring compacting boxplots down and using them as tab navigation.
The answer to the trade-offs I faced was to do away with the boxplot and compact everything down to thumbnail size, and use this as the navigation. Things started to feel like they were coming together.
Pros:
  • It answers the question, "What's the week like?", effectively enough.
  • Good wayfinding. It guides users to either specific dates or weather that might be of concern. Also, the compact forecasts give off a good scent—They convey the headline, then get the user to the details they might need.
  • It's compact and frees up space to allow room for detailed forecasts and charts.
Con:
  • It doesn't have the visual impact of box plots that clearly show the temperature trend for the week.
A Few Interesting Concepts
Below are a few early concepts that eventually made it into the prototype. These examples show how an iterative approach to design that includes data analysis can lead to novel solution ideas. It also shows how seemingly disparate ideas can come together in interesting ways.
Air Pollution Idea One
I started experimenting with puting air pollution forecasts line charts over color-coded qualitative categories defined by the EPA. These that convey air quality hazardousness with easy-to-understand labels such as "Unhealthy" and "Very Unhealthy".
Air Pollution Idea Two
In this version I made the qualitative color bars vertical. This would allow users to align unhealthy air quality with the time of day on the x-axis. (e.g., "Air quality will be unhealthy from 12 pm until 3 pm.") This resonated with what I saw in the data: air quality can go from good to unhealthy in just a few hours.
Chart Interactions
These sketches explore how a double tap will activate the chart and allow users to drag across it to see hourly values (answering the question "How cold will it be when I walk to work tomorow morning?"). When I work through interactions in the ealy stages of design, I often start with small, thumbnail sketches.
Historical Data
Above is one of many sketches exploring how historic data might be displayed. In this case, the gray-shaded area chart behind the forecast line shows 95th and 5th percentiles, which would show the user how cold or warm it can get. This concept ended up working well together with the chart interactions.
Wireframing
Mapping Out Flows
I took all the questions I found myself writing and grouped them into scenarios. These made clear that there were a few basic UX flows, each of which answering a handful of user needs.
Putting this all together brought clarity to the scope of the app and helped me start to think about architecture (technical and IA).
The UX flows broke out into sections, each of which answered questions such as "Is there any severe weather / air pollution coming up that affects me?" and, "Do I need to do anything (evacuate, stay indoors, avoid driving, etc.)?".
Prototyping
Code as Part of the Design Process
Designing for Many Screen Sizes
One of the benefits of React Native is its dev environment, called Expo, which enables "hot reloads". Using Expo meant that when I made a change to the code, I could see the effects immediately. I got in the habit of running three iOS simulators so I could work out layouts on many screen sizes at once.
Inspiration from Code
The feature that allows users to access detail historical data came to me when writing code. I created a quick sketch on paper then built it out.
The prototype running on an iPad. The screen shows a day with very poor air quality.
Scenarios
Key User Scenarios
Janice packs for a trip to New York City
Scenario: Janice is going to New York City for business and wants to get an idea of what the weather will be like while she's there.
She opens the app and adds New York City. She then checks the daily temperatures to see how cold it will be in the morning when she'll be walking to the office. Weather is volatile in New York, and forecasts can change. Janice checks historic data to see what is normal for the time of year.
Nothing looks too extreme, and there is no rain until the weekend, but she packs and umbrella just in case.
Key Questions / Goals:
  • What is the weather like for a particular city?
  • Will I need a coat when I'm walking to work tomorrow morning?
  • Will I need an umbrella?
Dave wants to go for a walk, but postpones due to air pollution alert
Scenario: Dave wants to go on a walk during his lunch break. He checks the weather and notices an air pollution alert. He clicks it and is quickly able to see the air is very unhealthy for the rest of the afternoon.
He sees that EPA guidance recommends staying indoors. He sees the air quality gets much better after 5 pm and decides to check the air quality later to see if its safe to be outside.
Key Questions / Goals:
  • Is there any severe weather / air pollution coming up that affects me?
  • When will there be severe weather / air pollution? For how long?
  • How severe? Do I need to worry?
  • Do I need to do anything (evacuate, stay indoors, avoid driving, etc.)?
Design Principles
A Few Key Design Principles
In addition to the design solving user needs (aka questions), a few design principles drove some of my thinking.
Put controls near the thing they affect
This is a standard Nielsen/Norman heuristic, but I have found it lacking in many mobile UIs.
Let users decide what's important
During fire season in California, air pollution if very important. Other times of year it isn't. Let users opt in to data sets that they need.
Let users recover from mistakes
Undo functionality lets users recover from accidentally deleting a city with one click. Recovering from deleting a city requires three steps. Why not make it one?
Conclusion
Learnings
  • React Native is powerful but challenging: Being able to instantly see changes to code in three iOS simulators was a nice way to work through UI layout rules. But React Native was also challenging to learn, especially animations (the hourly data exploration).
  • I'm on the fence about where and how code-based prototyping should fit in the design process: I feel like there is space for a prototyping tool like Principle to work through interactions. For example, I'm not sure I like the left/right swiping and might want to try a vertical swipe as an alternative. It's relatively difficult to explore in code.
  • Having a disciplined design process is important: Because this was a side project, I tried to short-circuit the design process, but found having some kind of evidence-based criteria for evaluating designs is crucial.
