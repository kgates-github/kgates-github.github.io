Prototyping a Mobile-First Data App
An Exploration in Data Visualization and React Native for Mobile Design
For this project, I wanted to augment my experience conveyed by my other case studies. My goal was to explore mobile design patterns while leveraging my background in data visualization. I also wanted to learn a bit of React Native.
In services of these objectives, I designed an app that combines weather and air pollution forecasts, and built it mobile-first from the ground up.
Screenshots of my weather forecast prototype on an iPhone 13 Max
Discovery and Exploration
Discovery and Exploration
Data Analysis and Sketching
I started off by exploring publicly-available data sets: The U.S. government’s weather service API, the EPA’s climate data and OpenWeather.com. I wanted to get a sense of what was out there and what I might include in my app.
I found myself drawn to two things I generally had not seen in weather apps: historical data and air pollution forecasts. But at this point, I wasn't sure what I might do with them.

Learning Through Iteration
As I iterated on the designs, I was able to suss out trade-offs between approaches. For example, I started with a boxplot chart showing highs and lows for the week. This answered the question, "What's the week going to be like?".
This approach visually conveyed the week's temperature trend, but the app needed to answer more specific questions such as, "How cold will it be tomorrow morning when I walk to work?".
The answer to the trade-offs I faced was to do away with the boxplot and compact everything down to thumbnail size, and use this as the navigation.
Sketches and wireframes showing how initial boxplot forecast shrunk and became the tab navigation.
Wireframing
Mapping Out Flows
I took all the questions I found myself writing and grouped them into scenarios. These made clear that there were a few basic UX flows, each of which answering a handful of user needs.
Putting this all together brought clarity to the scope of the app and helped me start to think about architecture (technical and IA).
The UX flows broke out into sections, each of which answered questions such as "Is there any severe weather / air pollution coming up that affects me?" and, "Do I need to do anything (evacuate, stay indoors, avoid driving, etc.)?".

Historical Data
Above is one of many explorations on how historic data might be displayed. In this case, the gray-shaded area chart behind the forecast line shows 95th and 5th percentiles, which would show the user how cold or warm it can get at a given time of year.

Users can see data for specific times of day by activating the chart with a long tap. Once activated, A drag-down interaction "pulls" historic temperature data out, answering the question, "How cold can it get this time of year in the morning?"
Prototyping
Code as Part of the Design Process
Designing for Many Screen Sizes
One of the benefits of React Native is its dev environment, called Expo, which enables "hot reloads". Using Expo meant that when I made a change to the code, I could see the effects immediately.
React Native uses FlexBox, which means layouts can be defined as rules that respond differently depending on screen dimensions and content. I got in the habit of running three iOS simulators so I could work out layouts on many screens at once.
The prototype running on an iPad. The screen shows a day with very poor air quality.
Scenarios
Key User Scenarios
Janice packs for a trip to Juneau, Alaska
Scenario: Janice is going to Juneau for business. She wants to get an idea of what the weather will be like while she's there so she knows what to pack.
She opens the app and adds Juneau. She then checks the daily temperatures to see how cold it will be in the morning when she'll be walking to the office. She sees it will be in the low 30s.
Weather is volatile in Alaska, and forecasts can change. Janice checks historic data to see what is normal for the time of year. She sees it can dip into the low 20s.
Janice gets her ski jacket out of the atttic and packs it along with an umbrella.
User Goals:
  • What is the weather like for a particular city?
  • Will I need a coat when I'm walking to work tomorrow morning? Will I need an umbrella?
  • How cold does it get in the morning this time of year?
Dave wants to go for a walk, but postpones due to air pollution alert
Scenario: Dave wants to go on a walk during his lunch break. He checks the weather and notices an air pollution alert. He clicks it and is quickly able to see the air is very unhealthy for the rest of the afternoon.
He sees that EPA guidance recommends staying indoors. He sees the air quality gets much better after 5 pm and decides to check the air quality later to see if it's safe to be outside.
User Goals:
  • Is there any severe weather / air pollution coming up that affects me?
  • When will there be severe weather / air pollution? For how long?
  • How severe? Do I need to worry?
  • Do I need to do anything (evacuate, stay indoors, avoid driving, etc.)?
Design Principles
A Few Key Design Principles...
In addition to the design solving user needs (aka questions), a few design principles drove some of my thinking.
Try to put controls near the thing they affect
This is a standard Nielsen/Norman heuristic, but I have found it lacking in many mobile UIs. I applied this principle to contextual help and changing between fahrenheit and celcius.
Let users decide what's important
During fire season in California, air pollution levels might determine if it's safe to be outside. Other times of the year it's not relevant. For people with allergies, pollen counts might be the first thing they check in the morning. Let users opt in to data sets that they need.
Let users recover from mistakes
Undo functionality lets users recover from accidentally deleting a city with one click. Recovering from deleting a city requires three steps. Why not make it one?
Conclusion
Learnings
  • React Native is powerful but challenging: Being able to instantly see changes to code in three iOS simulators was a nice way to work through UI layout rules. But React Native was also challenging to learn, especially animations (the hourly data exploration).
  • I'm on the fence about where and how code-based prototyping should fit in the design process: I feel like there is space for a prototyping tool like Principle to work through interactions. For example, I'm not sure I like the left/right swiping and might want to try a vertical swipe as an alternative. It's relatively difficult to explore in code.
  • Having a disciplined design process is important: Because this was a side project, I tried to short-circuit the design process, but found having some kind of evidence-based criteria for evaluating designs is crucial.
