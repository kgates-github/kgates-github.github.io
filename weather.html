Prototyping a Mobile-First Data App
A Crash Course in React Native and Mobile Design
The goal of this project was to exercise skills I haven't used in a while. The last time I designed for mobile was the Obama 2012 campaign, and, while I've written a lot of JavaScript (d3.js) working on a medical informatics team, that was years ago.
For this project, I wanted to explore mobile IX patterns while leveraging my background in data visualization. I also wanted to learn a bit of React Native.
Screenshots of the prototype running on an iPhone SE, iPhone 13 Mini, iPhone 13 Max, and a 12.9-inch iPad Pro.
Concept Exploration
Concept Exploration
Analysis and Sketching
I started off by exploring publicly-available data sets: The U.S. government’s weather service API, the EPA’s climate data and OpenWeather.com. I wanted to get a sense of what was out there and what I might include in my app.
I found myself drawn to two things I generally had not seen in weather apps: historical data and air pollution forecasts. But at this point, I wasn't sure what I might do with them.

"Competition"
I installed several weather apps on my phone and checked them out. I found most had confusing navigation / poor wayfinding. A few threw the kitchen sink at the user by offering way too much information. AccuWeather's data visualizations were appalling.
Sketching and Emergent User Scenarios
Once I started sketching concepts out on paper, I realzed something early on: I had a hard time reasoning about my concepts without some kind of criteria against which to evaluate feature ideas.
I started writing questions down on my drawing paper that I wanted the app to answer for users. These included things like “Will I need to bring a raincoat to work tomorrow?” and “Will it be warm enough tonight to eat dinner on the patio?”.
In early sketches, I started writing questions the app should be able to answer, such as "What's it like outside now?" and, "What is normal for this time of year?".
I realized the questions were filling the role that research findings would normally play in my regular design process. The questions also hung together in logical ways and groups of questions chained together formed something like journeys or user scenarios.
Early-Stage Concepts
Below are a few early concepts that eventually made it into the prototype.
Air Pollution Idea One
I started experimenting with puting air pollution forecasts line charts over color-coded qualitative categories defined by the EPA. These that convey air quality hazardousness with easy-to-understand labels such as "Unhealthy" and "Very Unhealthy".
Air Pollution Idea Two
The first iteration had the color coding arranged horizontally, but this version I made them vertical. This would allow users to align unhealthy air quality with the time of day on the x-axis. (e.g., "Air quality will be unhealthy from 12 pm until 3 pm."")
Chart Interactions
These sketches explore how a double tap will activate the chart and allow users to drag across it to see hourly values (answering the question "How cold will it be when I walk to work tomorow morning?"). When I work through interactions in the ealy stages of design, I often start with small, thumbnail sketches.
Historical Data
Above is one of many sketches exploring how historic data might be displayed. In this case, the gray-shaded area chart behind the forecast line shows 95th and 5th percentiles, which would show the user how cold or warm it can get. This concept ended up working well togther with the chart interactions.
Wireframing
Mapping Out Flows
I took all the questions I found myself writing and grouped them into scenarios. These made clear that there were a few basic UX flows, each of which answering a handful of user needs.
Putting this all together brought clarity to the scope of the app and helped me start to think about architecture (technical and IA).
The UX flows broke out into sections, each of which answered questions such as "Is there any severe weather / air pollution coming up that affects me?" and, "Do I need to do anything (evacuate, stay indoors, avoid driving, etc.)?".
Prototyping
Designing for Many Screen Sizes
One of the benefits of React Native is its dev enviroment, called Expo, which enables "hot reloads". Using Expo meant that when I made a change to the code, I could see the effects imediately. I got in the habit of running three iOS simulators so I could work out layouts on many screen sizes at once.
Scenarios
Key User Scenarios
Janice packs for a trip to New York City
Scenario: Janice is going to New York City for business and wants to get an idea of what the weather will be like. She adds New York City and checks the daily temperatures to see how cold it will be in the morning when she walks to the office. Nothing looks too extreme, and there is no rain until the weekend, but she packs and umbrella just in case.
Key Questions / Goals:
  • What is the weather like for a particular city?
  • Will I need a coat when I'm walking to work tomorrow morning?
  • Will I need an umbrella?
Dave decides to postpone a run due to air pollution
Scenario: Dave wants to go on a run. He checks the weather and notices an air pollution alert. He clicks it and is quickly able to see the air is very unhealthy for the rest of the afternoon. The EPA guidance recommends staying indoors. He sees the air quality gets much better after 5 pm and decides to check the air quality later to see if its okay to run.
Key Questions / Goals:
  • Is there any severe weather / air pollution coming up that affects me?
  • When will there be severe weather / air pollution? For how long?
  • How severe? Do I need to worry?
  • Do I need to do anything (evacuate, stay indoors, avoid driving, etc.)?
Put controls near the thing they affect
This is a standard Nielsen/Norman heuristic, but I have found it lacking in many mobile UIs.
I put the controls for toggling between fahrenheit and celsius next to temperature readings. (This was influenced by Google’s weather module, which has a lot of features I like.)
Embed usage tips in the UX
Generally good practice. Getting content and timing just right seems like it would take some iterations. I would really like to test these on users with no experience with my app.
