Location Targeting Obama's Achievements
A Hard Deadline (Election Day)
When we kicked off this project in June of 2012, we knew from research conducted by Obama's headquarters that voters respond favorably to talking points when they are about their own community.
While we had a big data set with information about the president's achievements, we didn't have much more than a vague direction to make something voter-facing and location-based. The election was four months away. We had no idea where we would end up.
Screenshot of the finished product shortly after launch. (Visual design by branding team.)
We would go on to launch on Obama's Twitter account five weeks before election day.
The app we came up with ended up reaching the president's 42 million Twitter followers, but the path getting their was not a straight line. We explored many ideas, and there were ups and downs. At one point, we had to completely change directions because of a Romney attack ad.
How it Works:
Step One
A voter gets a tweet from Obama inviting them to check out a map. They click it.
Step Two
The app detects the voter's location. In this case they are in Orange County, Florida.
Step Three
The app compiles a webpage about Obama's achievements, all tailored to Orange County, Florida.
The Challenge
Many Unknowns
At the start of the project, we knew making the president's achievements relevant to the user's location was to be key, but there was a lot in the air:
  • Lack of direction: What kind of app should we create? What should it do?
  • All-volunteer staff: How could we motivate volunteers to work under deadline?
My Role
Based in the San Francisco Technology Field Office, I led the ideation, wireframing and ran weekly presentations to campaign headquarters. In addition, I led the dev team, wrangled data, coordinated the efforts of volunteers, and wrote a good portion of the JavaScript and CSS.
Collaborators
I worked with a director of product management in the Chicago headquarters. Two engineers volunteered 8 - 10 hours a week, a dozen volunteers helped wrangle data, and the visual design was polished by the branding team.
Concept Development
Exploring the Data With Geospacial Tools
With campaign-provided data at hand and research findings in mind, I set out to better understand the shape of the data and what might be done with it. Ultimately, my goal was to come up with ideas to pitch to our stakeholders that would resonate with the campaign's goals.
I set up geospatial tools (PostgreSQL, PostGIS and QuantumGIS) and started exploring the data. I considerd a lot possibilities: children gaining health care; renewable energy investments; tax savings, and many more.
Examples of maps I created to explore the campaign's data sets using PostgreSQL, PostGIS and QuantumGIS.
Wireframing
Refining a Direction
Our stakeholders liked the direction of a recovery-themed map I created, which used data about the 2009 stimulus bill. We got the go-ahead to move forward.
The first wireframes showed stimulus projects color-coded by category. These categories corresponded to the campaign's core policy areas: education, energy, health and infrastructure.
Using Google's mapping API, we could detect the users location, and just show data points from their area.
The first wireframes I created show the top five recovery projects per state. Projects are color-coded to correspond to campaign themes: education, energy, health and infrastructure. The maps were created using real data.
As the user zooms in, more and more projects are revealed. At each zoom level, the most relevant projects are highlighted in the inset at the left.
People
Inspiring Volunteers
With a green light from headquarters, momentum started building. We would need engineers and since most of the data was uncategorized, we would need volunteers to label about 2,000 projects.
The question lingered: How could we motivate volunteers to help hit deadlines?
Assume intrinsic motivation
The engineers who volunteered were willing to go through an interview process, so they likely want to help the president get re-elected.
Act like shipping product is inevitable
One thing I know from my background as a developer is that engineers are fundamentally creative and largely motivated by bringing something new into the world. If it looks like we are going to ship, engineers will be more likely to help. Momentum then builds in a virtuous cycle.
Be clear about scope
Break work down into doable chunks. Be sure volunteers know exactly what is expected of them and when things need to be done.
A work book I created to let volunteers help categorize recovery projects by interpreting unformated text. We categorized more than 2,000 projects with the help of volunteers.
Pivot
Adapting to Chaos
In July, things abruptly changed for the worse. Just as the recovery map concept started to take shape, the Romney campaign attacked the stimulus bill. The Obama campaign banned the mention of it. The project was stopped in its tracks.
Campaign headquarters put the kibosh on our project. We were stopped in our tracks.
A New Direction
Everything could have easily ended at this point. Rather than give up, I went back and re-evaluated the data sets we hadn't used, and came up with a new solution.
The new concept detected the user's location and displayed the president's accomplishments across all of the campaign's policy areas. The digital director at headquarters liked the new direction, and gave us the green light.
Early version of our new direction. This concept pulls in relevant data in about health, education, tranportation etc.
Build
Going Mobile-First
Due to time constraints, I stopped wire framing and worked directly with the prototype we had started. Our goal was to have a fully functional app with just enough opinion about the UI. The branding team in Chicago would take it from there.
We wanted to make sure the app would work well on mobile. As we wrote the code and defined the UI, we tested constantly on several phones and tablets.
Using Twitter's Bootstrap UI framework, I was able to simultaneously design for tablets and phones, helping us keep to a very tight timeline.
One challenge we faced was that some county-level data points didn't exist. When that was the case, we show county data when we had it, and state data when we didn't. In other words, our app's logic mande the information displayed as local as possible.
An early version with all the working components. The app detects where the user is located and supplies talking points relevant to them across key policy areas.
The final version of the app after the branding team's work.
Conclusion
Launching on Obama's Twitter Account
The final weeks of the campaign were total chaos. Despite this, we managed to launch the morning of October 1st. Later that day, the campaign sent the URL out on Obama's Twitter account, where it reached the president's 42 million followers.
