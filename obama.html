About
Location Targeting Obama's Achievements
At the start, we knew from research conducted by Obama's headquarters that voters respond favorably to talking points when they are about their own community.
It was June 2012. The election was five months away. All we had was a pile of goverment data, a vague sense of direction, and a mandate to make something voter-facing.
The Challenge
A Nebulous Concept
We knew making the president's achievements relevant to the user's location was to be key, but that's about it. We didn't know what, how or with whom.
My Role
Based in the San Francisco Technology Field Office, I led the ideation, wireframing and ran weekly presentations to campaign headquarters. In addition, I led the dev team, wrangled data, coordinated the efforts of volunteers, and wrote a good portion of the JavaScript and CSS.
Collaborators
I worked closely with a director of product management in the Chicago headquarters. Two engineers volunteered 8 - 10 hours a week, a dozen volunteers helped wrangle data, and the visual design was polished by the branding team.
Screenshot of the finished product shortly after launch.
Project Atlas would go on to be launched on Obama's Twitter account five weeks before election day.

The app we came up with ended up reaching the president's 42 million Twitter followers, but the path getting their was not a straight line. We explored many possible directions. At one point, we had to completely change directions because of a Romney attack ad.
Despite all of this, we succeeded in the end. This is how we did it.
Concept Development
Finding stories in data
With campaign-provided data at hand and research findings in mind, I set out to better understand the shape of the data and what stories it might reveal. Ultimately, my goal was to come up with ideas to pitch to our stakeholders that would resonate with the campaign's goals.
I set up a geospatial database (PostgreSQL, PostGIS and QuantumGIS) and started making maps. I made a lot: children gaining health care; renewable energy investments; tax savings, and many more.
Examples of maps I created to explore the campaign's data sets.
Wireframing
Refining a direction
Our stakeholders liked the direction of a recovery-themed map I created, which used data about the 2009 stimulus bill. The first wireframes showed stimulus projects color-coded by category. These categories corresponded to the campaign's core policy areas: education, energy, health and infrastructure.
Since most of the data was uncategorized, we mobilized volunteers to label about 2,000 projects.
The first wireframes I created show the top five recovery projects per state. Projects are color-coded to correspond to campaign themes: education, energy, health and infrastructure. The maps were created using real data.
As the user zooms in, more and more projects are revealed. At each zoom level, the most relevant projects are highlighted in the inset at the left.
Pivot
Adapting to chaos
In July, things abruptly changed for the worse. Just as the recovery map concept started to take shape, the Romney campaign attacked the stimulus bill. The Obama campaign banned the mention of it. The project was stopped in its tracks.
Campaign headquarters put the kibosh on our project. We were stopped in our tracks.
Everything could have easily ended at this point. Rather than give up, I went back and re-evaluated the data sets we hadn't used, and came up with a new solution.
The new concept detected the user's location and displayed the president's accomplishments across all of the campaign's policy areas. The digital director at headquarters liked the new direction, and gave us the green light.
Early version of our new direction. This concept pulls in relevant data in about health, education, tranportation etc.
People Management
Inspiring volunteers
Once we had a green light, momentum started building. We would need staff. They question lingered: How could we motivate people to help hit tight deadlines without paying them?
Engineering
Assume intrinsic motivation
All the engineers volunteered and were willing to go through an interview process, so they likely want to help the president get re-elected.
Act like shipping product is inevitable
One thing I know from my background as a developer is that engineers are fundamentally creative and largely motivated by bringing something new into the world. If it looks like we are going to ship, engineers will be more likely to help. Momentum then builds in a virtuous cycle.
Crowd Sourcing
We needed volunteers to help us categorize more than 10,000 recovery projects by interpreting unformatted free text.
Assume intrinsic motivation
Like the engineers, we could infer that other volunteers want to help. Make sure they understand the why their contribution is important. When reaching out to volunteers, we were very clear about how their efforts would help the campaign.
Leverage the president's popularity
With 600,000 volunteers, we had numbers on our side. We cast our nets far and found people who are really motivated.
Be clear about scope
Break work down into doable chunks. Be sure volunteers know exactly what is expected of them and when things need to be done.
A work book I created to let volunteers help categorize recovery projects by reading unformated text. We categorized more than 10,000 projects with the help of a dozen volunteers.
